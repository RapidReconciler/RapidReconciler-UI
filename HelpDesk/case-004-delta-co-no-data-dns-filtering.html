Case 004 · 2026-02-23 · Path 3 (customer-side) · No card matched

System-wide “no data” — DNS filtering on the customer's network.

A customer-admin reports an urgent morning outage: every user across the whole system sees no data; the admin panel shows “no database connection.” The system was working the previous afternoon. The customer has already ruled out Citrix, JDE CNC, and certificates on their side. The diagnostic gold turned out to be the external-vs-internal DNS comparison — resolution worked from GSI's side and from the customer's external connections, but failed from inside their corporate network. Root cause was a recently-added DNS filtering policy on the customer's side that inadvertently blocked *.getgsi.com. The fix was on the customer's network; only their IT could apply it.

Section 2

The walkthrough — rule out our side, then guide the diagnosis on theirs.

Day 1
8:30 AM
arrives

Read the email. Two signals to anchor on.

Davis's email gives me two key signals before I open any tool:

(1) "All users" — this is system-wide, not user-specific. That rules out everything in the user-permission and per-account scenario library. Whatever's wrong is at infrastructure level.
(2) "Was working yesterday afternoon" — sudden onset, recent change. Something changed within the last ~16 hours.

The customer has also pre-empted three common suspects on their side — Citrix, JDE CNC, certificates — which is genuinely useful. They've also flagged one self-suspected change (a new company added yesterday afternoon). That's a candidate, but it's worth being skeptical of: a bad config typically fails at the moment of the change, not 12+ hours later. The "added at 3pm, broke this morning" pattern argues against config and for infrastructure.

Day 1
9:00 AM

Rule out GSI side first. Always. Don't assume customer-side until you've checked.

Quick internal pings:

· RR DBA: "Is the Delta Co RR database up? Any maintenance overnight?" — reply: up, healthy, no maintenance.
· RR Network Tech: "Anything network-side at our end? Outage on rrprod-<customer>.getgsi.com?" — reply: nothing, host responds normally to our internal monitoring.

So the issue isn't a GSI outage. From our side, the customer's environment looks healthy. That means we need to test from the customer's side — which means asking Davis (or his IT team) to run two specific tests.

Day 1
11:00 AM

Send the diagnostic email. Be specific about what to test and why.

The customer has volunteered useful information. The diagnostic email should match that level — tell them what to run, what to expect, and what the result will mean. The goal is one round of network tests that either confirms client-side or points us back at GSI.

Day 2
9:00 AM

Customer responds with the test results. The pattern is unambiguous.

Davis's reply confirms the diagnostic:

· nslookup from inside the network: returns nothing ("can't find rrprod-<customer>.getgsi.com").
· ping from inside the network: fails immediately with "host unknown".
· Same tests from a phone hotspot (their IT lead's iPhone): both succeed, hostname resolves to a valid IP, ping responds.

That's the signature of client-side network filtering. The hostname exists, our infrastructure responds normally, but something inside Delta Co's network is preventing the resolution from completing for users on the corporate network. This is on their side — specifically their DNS server, their corporate firewall, or their content-filtering layer. None of those are things GSI can touch.

9:30 AM

Hand off to customer IT with a clean recommendation list.

The handoff email needs to do three things: state the diagnosis plainly, name the most likely causes (so the customer's IT knows where to look first), and reassure the customer that as soon as they fix the network side, RR will work normally without any change on our end.

To:D. Davis
CC:Delta Co IT Helpdesk
Subject:RE: URGENT — Rapid Reconciler showing no data for all users
Hi, Thanks for running those tests — that confirms the diagnosis. The fact that nslookup and ping both fail from inside your corporate network but succeed from outside (phone hotspot) means the issue is on your side: somewhere in your network, the GSI hostname rrprod-.getgsi.com is being filtered or not resolved. This is not something we can fix from our end — the host is responding normally and DNS is correct globally. Your IT team will need to investigate. Common causes for this exact pattern, in roughly order of frequency: 1. Recent DNS-filtering policy change. Many corporate networks run DNS-based content filtering (Cisco Umbrella, internal DNS deny-lists, etc.). A new rule or category that catches *.getgsi.com would produce exactly this symptom. 2. Recent firewall rule change. An outbound block rule catching traffic to GSI's IP range, or to *.getgsi.com. 3. Internal DNS server config drift. Stale or incorrect records on your internal DNS server for our domain. Given the issue started yesterday afternoon and was working before that, your IT team should look at any network or DNS policy changes made in the last 24-48 hours. Once they identify and lift the block, RR will come back online immediately — no action needed on our end or in the application. Please loop us in when your IT has had a chance to look. We'll keep this ticket open until you confirm it's resolved. Thanks, Sam GSI Helpdesk

Section 3

Resolution & lessons — what the customer's IT found, what made this case different.

What the customer's IT found

A DNS filtering policy added two days earlier inadvertently blocked *.getgsi.com.

Delta Co runs a corporate DNS-filtering layer (a typical setup for a finance-team-heavy environment). Two days before the outage, their security team had added a new category to the deny-list as part of a routine policy refresh. The category included a domain pattern that matched *.getgsi.com, so internal DNS lookups for our hostname returned no record from that point on. The block was caught immediately when their IT team looked at recent policy changes — a one-line removal in the filtering console restored DNS resolution and RR came back up for all users without any change on the GSI side or in the RR application itself. The "new company added in RR" was a coincidence in timing, not a cause.

What this case taught us
  • “No database connection” is often a downstream symptom — test the network layer first when you see it suddenly. The admin panel error is technically accurate (the app can't reach the DB) but the upstream cause was network. Multi-tier apps fail in misleading ways: when the bottom layer breaks, every layer above it shows whatever “something didn't work” error its developers wrote. Don't take the error message at face value — check whether the host is reachable at all before assuming the database is the problem.
  • “All users, started suddenly, was working yesterday” → infrastructure, not config. A config bug surfaces at the moment the config changes, not 12+ hours later. A “sudden onset, affects everyone” pattern points at infrastructure: network, DNS, firewall, server uptime, certificate expiry, scheduled maintenance. Always reach for that bucket first when the timeline shows that shape, even when the customer self-suspects a recent application-level change like “we added a new company.”
  • External-vs-internal DNS comparison is the diagnostic gold for client-side network issues. One round of nslookup + ping from inside the customer's network, plus the same from outside (phone hotspot is enough), gives you a definitive answer in five minutes. If external resolves and internal doesn't, the issue is unambiguously on the customer's side. This is faster and cheaper than any other diagnostic for this class of issue.
  • The customer-side handoff is its own path. It's Path 3, not Path 4. Path 4 means escalating inside GSI — to RR Network Tech, RR DBA, or RR Developer. When the diagnosis points at the customer's own network, you've done research-and-diagnosis work (Path 3) and the action sits on their side. The reply needs to do two things: state the diagnosis plainly enough that the customer's IT can act on it, and list the most likely culprits in priority order so they don't have to research from scratch. That's the structural difference between this case and Case 001 (which was Path 4 — routed inside GSI to Network Tech).
  • Customer-volunteered "what we ruled out" is gold — reinforce it loudly. Davis listed Citrix, CNC, certs as already-checked. Acknowledging that explicitly in the first reply (“thanks especially for ruling those out”) builds trust during an outage and signals that we're treating their work as serious. If a customer comes back with a self-diagnosis we end up confirming, the reply structure above doubles as a template for that pattern.
  • Documentation gaps surfaced. Provisional new Tier 1 scenario card: “System-wide ‘no data’ / ‘no database connection’ suddenly → first test is external-vs-internal DNS resolution; if external works and internal doesn't, recommend customer IT review their DNS / firewall / DNS-filtering policy for changes in the last 24-48 hours.” New reply templates needed: diagnostic-network-test-request (the Day-1 email above) and customer-side-network-handoff (the Day-2 email). Both are reusable as-is. Status: open.
