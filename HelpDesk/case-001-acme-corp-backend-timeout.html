Section 2

The walkthrough — click by click, six minutes.

9:47 AM
arrives

Read the email before opening any tool.

The CC line is what I check first. No GSI engineer is on it — just the customer's teammate and their internal service desk. So I'm not joining a thread that's already in flight on our side. I am the first GSI eyes on this.

The subject is RE: INC<XXXXXXX>. That's their ServiceNow ticket, not ours. The "RE:" hints there's prior history in the chain — I scroll up. No earlier GSI replies. Pedro is reaching out cold.

Pedro's message is short and helpful: he tested inside and outside his company network and got the same failure. That's exactly the test a Helpdesk Tech would have asked him to run, and he ran it himself before reaching out. Do not send him VPN steps. Fastest way to lose his trust.

9:48 AM

Open the attached screenshot. The diagnostic is here.

The error banner reads: Connect to <gsi-backend>.cloudapp.net:<port> [<gsi-backend>.cloudapp.net/<azure-ip>] failed: connect timed out.

Three things this tells me without insider knowledge:

(1) DNS is fine. The hostname resolved to an IP address — if DNS were broken the bracket would be empty or the message would say ERR_NAME_NOT_RESOLVED.
(2) TCP connect to that port is timing out. The hostname routes somewhere, but nothing is listening on that port or the path is blocked.
(3) *.cloudapp.net is Azure. That's GSI hosting, not the customer's. Combined with Pedro's cross-network test, the issue cannot be on his side.

This is not a Path 2 fix. Move to Path 4.

9:49 AM

I open troubleshooting.html in a new tab. Click the Email input card. Paste Pedro's message into the textarea. Click Find matching cards.

Top hits: customer-no-data-vpn and customer-browser-local-network-access. Both already excluded by his cross-network test. I close those.

Best match is ui-loads-agent-not-connecting — Tier 2, blue. Close enough — the static UI loaded but the backend isn't responding. I open the detail pane. It reads: route to RR Network Tech with the screenshot. That's the play.

Section 3

Resolution & lessons — what Network Tech found, what made this case different.

Note: The resolution paragraph below is illustrative — representing the most plausible cause for this symptom. Replace with the actual finding once Network Tech reports back.
What Network Tech found (illustrative)

Backend Network Security Group rule had drifted.

The Azure NSG protecting <gsi-backend>.cloudapp.net had been updated as part of an earlier maintenance change and the inbound allow-rule for the customer-facing port had been narrowed unintentionally. The DNS record was healthy and the underlying VM was running — the firewall in front of it was the culprit, which is why the symptom presented as a connect timeout rather than a name-resolution or 5xx error. NSG rule was restored; service available within ~30 minutes of escalation.

What this case taught us
  • The customer doing the cross-network test pre-emptively saved us a round-trip. Pedro is not the first customer to do this, but he's the first one we've documented doing it. Acknowledging that test loudly in the reply ("thanks especially for running the cross-network test") is what kept his trust intact during an outage.
  • No scenario card matched cleanly. The closest existing card was ui-loads-agent-not-connecting, but its assumed cause (agent disconnect after install) doesn't match what was actually wrong (backend NSG drift). This case is the seed for a new Tier 2 card — provisional title: "Login fails: connect timeout to GSI backend — possible NSG / backend availability issue."
  • The Tier 1 reply library doesn't cover "customer self-diagnosed, escalating" yet. Sam free-wrote the reply in step 5. With volume this low it's fine, but this is the obvious 13th reply template — customer-already-self-diagnosed-escalate — if the pattern recurs.
  • Knowing what runs on each Azure backend hostname matters at escalation time. Sam pasted the error verbatim, which was correct, but a small "GSI Backend Reference" page listing each *.cloudapp.net host, what it serves, and what port it listens on would let any Tier 1 person tell Network Tech which service is timing out, not just that something is. That's a tech-team reference doc only Edward can write.
