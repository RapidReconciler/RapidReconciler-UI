What RapidReconciler Does (and Doesn’t)

Five minutes of orientation. The aim is to translate what you already know about JD Edwards transfer orders into where it lives and what it’s called inside RapidReconciler — and to set boundaries on what RR can and cannot change.

For everyone. Both warehouse personnel and inventory accountants benefit from this orientation. Skip nothing.

The Read-Only Model

RapidReconciler does not write to JDE. It reads from JDE on a nightly import cycle, compares the data, and surfaces discrepancies. Every correction — every receipt entry, every quantity adjustment, every JE — is posted in JD Edwards using the tools you already know. RapidReconciler then picks up the change at the next nightly refresh.

RapidReconciler can changeOnly JD Edwards can change
Notes attached to an order pair; exclusions on a pair; filter and view preferences; report exports. F4211 (sales orders), F4311/F43121 (purchase orders and receipts), F0902 (GL balances), shipment and receipt records, item master — everything that matters for transfer reconciliation.

The practical implication: if a missed receipt is posted in JDE at 3pm, RR won’t reflect the receipt until the next morning’s import. The exclusion mechanism (which is in RR) is what removes a pair from the variance calculation in the meantime if it’s known to be unresolvable.

What It’s Comparing

The headline question RapidReconciler answers for In Transit is: does the sum of all open transfer order pair values agree to the In-Transit GL clearing account balance?

In a clean environment, every shipment (ST) is eventually offset by a matching receipt (OT), the order pair’s TranQty and TranAmt fall to zero, and that pair stops contributing to the In-Transit balance. In practice, certain pairs never clear — cost variances between branches, items shipped against the wrong OT, partial receipts that nobody completed, transfers cancelled mid-flight. Those are the items the inventory accountant investigates.

Order Pairs Explained

The single most important concept in In-Transit reconciliation. If you understand order pairs, almost everything else on the screen follows. If you don’t, the rest of the doc will feel arbitrary.

For everyone. Warehouse personnel use this to verify their transfers; inventory accountants use it to investigate variances. Same concept, different uses.

What Is an Order Pair?

In JD Edwards, a transfer between two branches creates two separate orders: an ST sales order on the source branch (the goods leaving) and an OT purchase order on the destination branch (the goods arriving). RapidReconciler reads both, matches them using the related order linkage on the F4211 record, and presents them as a single row in the In Transit module.

AspectST (shipping side)OT (receiving side)
JDE table F4211 (sales order detail) F4311/F43121 (PO detail / receipts)
Branch Source — where goods leave from Destination — where goods arrive
Records The shipment The receipt
Result One ST + One OT + One Item = One row in the In Transit module

The pair is summarized at the item number level. If a single transfer moves multiple items, each item produces its own pair — one row per ST/OT/item combination.

The Two Numbers That Matter

Once paired, RapidReconciler calculates the in-transit position for each pair by subtracting receipts from shipments:

FieldCalculationWhat It Means
TranQty Ship Qty − Rec Qty Quantity still in transit between the two branches.
TranAmt Ship Amt − Rec Amt Dollar value still sitting in the In Transit clearing account.

When TranQty and TranAmt are both zero, the pair is fully cleared — quantity shipped equals quantity received, and the dollar value has cleared the In-Transit account. Any non-zero value means there’s open transfer activity contributing to the In-Transit balance.

The Three States of an Order Pair

For quick mental reference, every pair you encounter is in one of these three states:

StateTranQtyWhat It Means
Cleared = 0 (and TranAmt = 0) Goods received as expected. Pair is no longer contributing to In-Transit balance. Warehouse — this is what success looks like.
Open (in flight) > 0 Goods shipped but not yet received. Normal between shipment and receipt — if the gap is days rather than weeks, this is fine.
Variance = 0 but TranAmt ≠ 0 Quantity matched but dollars don’t. Cost difference between source and destination — the pair will never clear without action. Inventory Accountant needs to investigate.

Why This Matters

  • The In-Transit balance is the sum of every open pair’s TranAmt. The total at the bottom of the Orders page rolls up directly into the headline In-Transit balance shown on the Reconciliation page.
  • Exclusions happen at the pair level. When a reconciler excludes a pair, they’re telling RapidReconciler to stop counting that one ST/OT/item combination toward the In-Transit balance.
  • Cost variances surface at the pair level. If the ST sales price doesn’t equal the OT purchase cost on the same item, the pair will show TranQty = 0 but TranAmt ≠ 0 — a quantity match with a dollar mismatch.
  • Open pairs are the source of every variance. Whatever shows up on the Reconciliation page can be traced back to one or more open pairs on the Orders page.
i

Note on terminology. Although this guide refers to ST/OT order types, RapidReconciler detects different transfer types based on a flag in the F4211 table. The pairing logic is the same regardless of the specific order type codes used in your environment.

Your Daily Practice

How RapidReconciler fits into a typical day for warehouse personnel who ship or receive transfers between branches. The aim isn’t to make you a reconciler — it’s to give you enough RR awareness to confirm your work and to flag the things that need someone else’s attention.

For warehouse personnel. Inventory accountants should still skim this topic — understanding what warehouse staff see (and don’t) explains many of the variances you’ll investigate.

Where Your Work Shows Up

Every shipment and every receipt you process in JD Edwards eventually appears in RapidReconciler the morning after the nightly import. Specifically:

  • When you ship — goods leave the source branch, the ST sales order updates, and the pair’s TranQty (still in transit) increases. Until the matching receipt is processed, the dollars sit in the In-Transit GL account.
  • When you receive — goods arrive at the destination branch, the OT purchase order updates, and the pair’s TranQty drops back toward zero. When TranQty reaches zero and TranAmt also reaches zero, the pair has fully cleared.
  • When something goes wrong — partial receipt, wrong OT receipted against, cost difference between branches — the pair stays open in RR until somebody investigates it.

What to Do When a Transfer Is Complete

For most transfers, no RR action is needed — the pair clears automatically when the receipt posts in JDE. But verifying that the pair actually cleared is worth ten seconds, and saves the inventory accountant from chasing it later.

#ActionWhat To Look For
1 Open RR. Navigate to the In Transit module → Orders page.
2 Search by ST or OT number. Use whichever you have at hand — either side of the pair finds the same row.
3 Check TranQty and TranAmt. Both should be zero if the transfer is fully complete. If only TranQty is zero but TranAmt isn’t, you have a cost variance — flag it (see below).
4 If both are zero, you’re done. The pair has cleared cleanly. No further action needed.

When to Flag Something

Three situations warrant raising a hand to your inventory accountant or supervisor before period close. Each one means “this pair will probably never clear by itself.”

SignalWhat It Likely MeansWho to Tell
TranQty matches, TranAmt doesn’t. The ST cost and OT cost are different — usually a frozen-cost difference between branches. The pair will never clear without a JE. Inventory accountant.
Partial receipt sitting open for more than a few days. Either short-shipped, items damaged, or the receiver didn’t complete the receipt. The pair won’t clear until someone resolves the missing quantity. Receiving supervisor first; inventory accountant if unresolved by week-end.
You shipped to the wrong OT, or received against the wrong OT. An orphaned shipment with no match, or a receipt that crosses pairs. RR will show two pairs that won’t clear individually. Inventory accountant — this typically requires JDE-side cleanup that warehouse staff shouldn’t self-correct.
Try this now

If you have RR access and you’ve shipped or received any transfer this week, look it up in the Orders page using its ST or OT number. Confirm whether TranQty and TranAmt are zero. If both are zero, you’ve seen what success looks like.

If they’re not zero and shouldn’t be (the receipt should have posted by now), that’s already actionable information — either there’s a real receiving issue, or the import hasn’t caught up yet (recent activity may take until tomorrow morning to appear).

What Not to Do

A short list, mostly to prevent well-intentioned over-correction:

  • Don’t make corrective receipts in JDE without a paper trail. If a receipt was missed, post it through normal procedure with proper documentation. Improvised “cleanup” receipts make inventory accountants’ lives hard a month later.
  • Don’t apply exclusions in RR. Exclusions are an inventory-accountant tool. Even if you have access to the Edit Note popup, leave the exclusion checkbox alone unless the inventory accountant explicitly asks you to act.
  • Don’t adjust costs to make a variance disappear. Cost variances mean something — usually a frozen cost out of date on one branch. Hiding the variance hides the configuration issue that caused it.
See Common Mistakes for the operational errors that cause the most In-Transit variances and how to prevent them.

Common Mistakes

The top operational errors that cause In-Transit variances. Most of these can be prevented with attention at the moment of shipping or receipt — far easier than fixing them after the period closes.

For warehouse personnel. Inventory accountants should also read this so they can recognize the patterns when investigating later.

Multiple Orders for the Same Item

The most common cause of unclearable variances. Two transfer orders move the same item from the same source branch to the same destination branch, around the same time. The receiver, in good faith, receives the full quantity against just one of the OTs — leaving the other OT with no receipt activity at all.

What you see in RR: two pairs for the same item. One pair has TranQty < 0 (over-received) and TranAmt that doesn’t reconcile. The other pair has TranQty > 0 (still “in transit”) even though the goods physically arrived weeks ago. Neither pair will clear without intervention.

How to prevent: when receiving, scan the OT number on the paperwork against what you’re receipting. Don’t default to the most-recent OT. If two OTs exist for the same item from the same source, both need quantity allocated — do not over-receipt one to clear the other.

Receiving Against the Wrong OT

A close cousin of the above, but different mechanism. The receiver records the receipt against an OT that doesn’t correspond to the actual goods that arrived. This typically happens when an OT is closed early or when paperwork mismatches.

What you see in RR: the wrong-OT pair shows TranQty < 0 (received more than was shipped against this pair); the right-OT pair stays open indefinitely. Two unresolvable pairs from one mistake.

How to prevent: the OT number on the receiving paperwork must match the OT being receipted against in the JDE screen. Treat any mismatch as a stop signal — raise it before completing the receipt, not after.

Partial Shipments Left Open

Goods are short-shipped (broken, missing, returned to stock at the source). The shipper doesn’t close out the ST after the partial shipment, so RapidReconciler still expects more goods to arrive at the destination — even though no more are coming.

What you see in RR: a pair where TranQty > 0 indefinitely. Looks like “in transit” but is actually “abandoned.”

How to prevent: short shipments must be closed in JDE. The remaining quantity needs to be returned to source-branch inventory through the proper transaction. Leaving the ST open isn’t a clean record-keeping shortcut — it creates a permanent open pair that someone has to investigate.

Cost Differences Between Branches

The ST sales price is calculated using the source branch’s cost; the OT purchase cost is calculated using the destination branch’s cost. If those costs differ — common when frozen costs are out of date on one branch or item costs were recently rolled on only one branch — the dollars don’t match even when the quantities do.

What you see in RR: TranQty = 0 (full quantity received), but TranAmt ≠ 0 (residual dollars sitting in the In-Transit account).

This is not a warehouse error. It’s a configuration issue with the cost setup. Flag it to your inventory accountant — they’ll either address it through a standard cost roll, post a JE, or apply an exclusion depending on materiality. Do not change costs in JDE to make the variance go away.

UOM Mismatches

The ST is created in one unit of measure (cases) and the receipt is processed in another (each), without RapidReconciler being able to correctly resolve the conversion. Less common than the above but particularly painful when it happens because the variance can be very large.

What you see in RR: a pair where TranQty looks wildly wrong — orders of magnitude different from what physically moved.

How to prevent: standardize on one UOM for transfer documentation per item. If both UOMs are valid in your environment, the JDE administrator needs to ensure UOM conversion factors are correctly configured for the affected items.

Your First Period-End Close

The transition from monitoring In-Transit variances day-to-day to closing and posting at period end. The procedure is fully documented in the Walkthrough — Step 5 (Investigate Transactions), Step 6 (Post Journal Entries), and Step 7 (Run Integrity Reports). This topic covers the things around the procedure.

For inventory accountants. Warehouse-only readers: this topic doesn’t apply to your role.

Two Recommendations Before You Close Live

Neither of these is enforced by the application. Both are worth the time.

Pair With an Experienced Reviewer for the First Close

Have someone who has closed In-Transit before sit with you (or be on a call) for your first end-to-end period close. Their job is to catch the small judgment calls the documentation can’t capture — especially the exclusion-or-not decisions on real pairs, and the materiality threshold your team applies to cost variances.

Walk a Closed Prior Period Before Closing Live

Before your first live close, walk a closed prior period in the application without changing anything. Look at which pairs were excluded, read the audit-trail notes, see what the closing JE looked like. The reasoning behind exclusion decisions is the part that doesn’t come from JDE experience — observing how your team has handled it before is the fastest way to internalize the local standard.

Pre-Close Checklist

Before you post the closing journal entry, confirm:

  • Pre-close requirements
  • The latest import has succeeded; status indicators on the Reconciliation page are green.
  • All pairs that should clear through normal shipping/receiving have done so — warehouse activity for the period is reflected in RR.
  • Each remaining unresolvable pair has been reviewed and either excluded with a defensible audit-trail note or scheduled for JDE-side correction.
  • The aggregate exclusion total on the Reconciliation page ties to the GL clearing JE you’re about to post.
  • Integrity Report 4 (cost variances) has been reviewed; cost-difference pairs are either being addressed via standard cost roll or will be excluded.

Top Three Mistakes New Inventory Accountants Make

Drawn from the Walkthrough’s Best Practices and Troubleshooting sections, narrowed to the three that catch first-time closers.

!

1. Excluding open orders that are simply slow to clear. An exclusion is for pairs that will never clear — cost differences, abandoned partial shipments, mis-receipted activity. If a pair is just “in transit longer than usual,” wait. The receipt may post tomorrow. Excluding it now means having to unexclude and re-include it after the receipt arrives, which compounds work and creates audit complexity.

!

2. Not reversing the closing JE when an exclusion is later corrected. If you exclude a pair and post a JE to clear its dollar value, and then the underlying issue is resolved in JDE (the missed receipt finally posts, the cost is rolled, etc.), the exclusion needs to be reversed and the original JE needs to be reversed. Forgetting the JE reversal leaves the GL out of balance with the next period’s reconciliation. The unexclude/re-exclude procedure in the Exclusion Guide covers this.

!

3. Posting on stale data. Same as the inventory and A/P close procedures — if the import status is yellow or red, the data isn’t ready. Wait for green before producing your closing JE. The cost of a delayed close is small; the cost of a closed period that doesn’t actually agree to JDE is much larger.

If Your First Close Has a Non-Zero In-Transit Variance After Posting

It happens. The Audit Report tells you what was excluded, what JEs were posted, and what the balances looked like at posting time. Review with your buddy. Most causes:

  • A pair contributed to the variance that should have been excluded but wasn’t.
  • An exclusion was applied but the matching JE was not posted (or vice versa).
  • The post-JE nightly refresh hadn’t completed when the variance was checked — check again the next morning.
  • New shipping or receiving activity posted in JDE between the close and the import.

None of these is a failure of the close itself. They’re learning events. Document what happened in your team’s notes — the second close benefits enormously from the first one’s post-mortem.

See The Exclusion Decision for the judgment framework on when to exclude vs. wait.

The Exclusion Decision

Excluding a pair removes it from the In-Transit balance and clears it from the unreconciled view. It’s the most consequential action available in the In Transit module. This topic covers when to exclude, when not to, and what happens after.

For inventory accountants. Warehouse personnel should not apply exclusions; this topic explains why — and gives inventory accountants the framework for making the call.

What Exclusion Does

When you exclude a pair, RapidReconciler stops counting that pair’s TranAmt toward the In-Transit balance. The pair stays visible in a filtered view (excluded items don’t disappear), but it no longer affects the headline variance and no longer shows up on the unreconciled Orders page by default.

What exclusion does not do:

  • It doesn’t modify F4211 (the ST), F4311/F43121 (the OT), or any GL data. The underlying JDE records are unchanged.
  • It doesn’t prevent future activity on the order. If a receipt is later posted in JDE against the excluded pair, the receipt flows through normally — and if it changes the pair’s state, you’ll see it via the unexclude/re-exclude procedure.
  • It doesn’t substitute for fixing data integrity issues. If the GL is wrong, exclusion doesn’t correct it — a JE is still required.

When to Exclude

Exclude a pair only when it meets all three of these conditions:

  1. The pair will never clear through normal shipping/receiving.
    • Cost difference between branches that won’t resolve via cost roll.
    • Receipt was posted against the wrong OT and the JDE-side correction would create more issues than it solves.
    • Partial shipment was abandoned (short-shipped, items never recovered) and source-branch inventory was already adjusted.
    • Installation-era pair from before RR went live, which can’t be reconstructed.
  2. The GL is correct as-is, or will be corrected by the JE that accompanies the exclusion.
    • If the GL is wrong, the JE comes first. Exclusion without a corresponding GL correction just hides the variance.
  3. The audit-trail note is defensible.
    • What the issue is, why removing the pair from variance is acceptable, the JE or ticket reference, the date, and your initials.
    • If you can’t write the note, you shouldn’t apply the exclusion.

When Not to Exclude

SituationWhy Exclusion Is the Wrong Tool
Pair is open but the receipt is just delayed. Wait for the next import. Most pairs that look stuck are in flight. Excluding them now creates an unexclude burden later.
You’re trying to make a variance go away under deadline pressure. If you can’t articulate why the pair will never clear, you’re hiding a real issue. The variance will reappear next period in a worse form.
The cost variance is large and there’s a chance the cost can be rolled. Talk to the cost accountant first. If a standard cost roll can correct the discrepancy across all affected pairs, that’s a cleaner fix than excluding each pair individually.
You’re uncertain whether the pair is unresolvable or just slow. Wait one more period. The cost of waiting is one period of variance reporting; the cost of excluding prematurely is post-mortem cleanup if the receipt does arrive.
The customer doesn’t have a documented materiality threshold yet. Establish the threshold first (with the controller). Without one, exclusion decisions vary by reviewer and audit reviews become contentious.

The Risk of Excluding an Open Order

This deserves its own emphasis because new inventory accountants often miss it. When you exclude a pair where TranQty ≠ 0 (still “in transit”), and a receipt later posts in JDE for that pair, RapidReconciler doesn’t automatically notice that the situation has changed. The exclusion stays in place; the new receipt activity sits inside the excluded pair, invisible by default.

The unexclude/re-exclude procedure in the Exclusion Guide exists precisely to handle this: each period close, the inventory accountant reviews previously-excluded pairs for new activity and acts accordingly. If you exclude an open pair, you’re committing to that ongoing review — not just signing off on the current period.

Vocabulary

RapidReconciler-specific terms relevant to In-Transit work, plus a few JDE terms that get used differently here than elsewhere. Each entry is a one-line definition plus a link to the doc that explains it in depth.

Exclusion (In Transit)
An action that removes a single ST/OT order pair from the In-Transit balance and unreconciled view. Different from an exclusion in the PO Receipts module — same word, different mechanism, different scope. The Exclusion Decision.
Exclusion (PO Receipts) — for disambiguation
A line-level annotation that removes a single PO line from the RNV variance calculation. Lives in the PO Receipts module, not here. If a colleague says “exclusion” without context, ask which module they mean. Suspensions & Exclusions.
Exclusion Adjust
An entry shown on the In Transit As-Of view representing the dollar amount removed from the In-Transit balance via exclusions. Lets reviewers see at a glance how much of the balance is “real” in-transit activity vs. excluded.
Order Pair
The fundamental unit of In-Transit reconciliation. One ST sales order + one OT purchase order + one item number = one row in the In Transit module. Order Pairs Explained.
OT (Other Order Type)
The purchase order side of a transfer pair — created on the destination branch, records the receipt. Whether your environment uses literal “OT” or a different document type code, the role is the same.
Recalc Balance button
A button on the Orders page that forces RapidReconciler to recompute TranQty and TranAmt for the displayed pairs. Useful when you’ve just posted activity in JDE and want to verify the impact, but normal automatic recalculation handles most cases.
Source Branch / Destination Branch
The two endpoints of a transfer. Source = where goods leave from (the ST side). Destination = where goods arrive (the OT side). Costs may differ between the two branches, which is the most common cause of pure dollar variances.
ST (Sales Transfer Order Type)
The sales order side of a transfer pair — created on the source branch, records the shipment. Whether your environment uses literal “ST” or a different document type code, the role is the same.
TranAmt
Ship Amt − Rec Amt for an order pair. The dollar value still sitting in the In-Transit clearing account for that pair. The In-Transit balance is the sum of TranAmt across every open pair.
TranQty
Ship Qty − Rec Qty for an order pair. The quantity still in transit between source and destination. Zero means the receipt fully matched the shipment in quantity.
Unexclude / Re-exclude procedure
The workflow for handling a previously-excluded pair that has had new JDE activity. Remove the exclusion, review the new state, then re-apply the exclusion (or not) based on the updated facts. Transfer Order Exclusion Guide.
Worked Transactions Filter
A filter option on the Transactions investigation screen (Walkthrough Step 5) that lets inventory accountants hide transactions that have already been reviewed and noted. Helps focus on the unreviewed remainder during a long investigation.
