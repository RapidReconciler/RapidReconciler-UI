Stock Valuation Report total differs from RR

You ran the JDE Stock Valuation Report for a period and the total doesn’t match what RapidReconciler shows on the Inventory As-of view for the same period. Almost always one of two things: the two reports were taken at different moments (timing), or they’re computing slightly different quantities (method).

Why the totals can legitimately differ

The two reports answer slightly different questions:

  • JDE Stock Valuation Report — a snapshot of inventory value at the moment you ran the report. It pulls the current quantity-on-hand from the JDE item branch tables and applies the current item cost.
  • RR Inventory As-of view — a reconstruction of inventory value as of a date you specify, built from the cardex transactions (F4111) and roll-forward / roll-back logic. It’s designed to give you a consistent “point in time” figure that survives subsequent activity.

If the JDE report is taken hours, days, or weeks apart from the RR view you’re comparing it to, the totals won’t match. That’s expected, not a bug.

How rounding causes a residual amount

Even with the same data and the same as-of moment, the two reports can disagree by a few cents per line because of where the rounding happens in the math.

In JDE, item quantities can carry 0 to 6 decimal places and unit costs are typically stored to 4 decimals. When the Stock Valuation Report multiplies these together, the extended value is rounded to 2 decimals for display. The Stock Valuation Report uses one fresh quantity × unit cost calculation per line at report time. RapidReconciler, by contrast, summarizes the cardex transaction amounts the way they were posted — each transaction was already rounded to 2 decimals when JDE wrote it, so the running total is a sum of pre-rounded values, not a single multiplication done at report time.

Worked example

An item with quantity = 3.142857 on hand and unit cost = 12.3456:

  • JDE Stock Valuation: 3.142857 × 12.3456 = 38.79989952, rounded to display as $38.80.
  • RR Inventory As-of: if the on-hand of 3.142857 was built from three separate transactions (each rounded to 2 decimals when posted), the summed amount might land at $38.81 — a $0.01 residual from the same underlying movement.

Across thousands of items the residuals accumulate to a small but real difference in the totals.

The most visible expression of this is a line on the As-of view that shows zero quantity but a non-zero residual amount — the cardex transactions for that item zeroed out the quantity perfectly but left a few cents of cumulative rounding behind.

Why we don’t hide these lines

Some customers ask us to suppress the zero-quantity-with-residual lines from the As-of details view. We don’t, because removing them creates a mismatch between the total on the Inventory Reconciliation page and the total on the As-of details page for the same period. Other customers explicitly want these residuals visible because they investigate them as part of their close. Both views always tie out by design.

How JDE handles these residuals (and why some still show up)

JDE has its own mechanism for clearing residual value at zero quantity: Zero Balance Adjustments (ZBAs). When a triggering transaction drives on-hand to zero and leaves residual cost behind, JDE auto-generates a journal entry that writes off the residual to a designated account (configured via DMAAI 4126 / 4128 for Inventory, 4400 / 4405 for Purchasing). The cardex row for the ZBA shows “Zero Balance Adjustment” in the Explanation field.

ZBAs only fire on specific transaction types, though:

  • Triggers a ZBA: inventory issue (P4112), inventory transfer (P4113), negative inventory adjustment (P4114), negative-quantity PO receipt or receipt reversal (P4312).
  • Does NOT trigger a ZBA: sales shipments (P4205 / R42800), inventory reclassification (P4116), cycle / tag count updates (R41413 / R41610), future cost updates (R41052), cost revisions (P4105).

That last bullet is the gap. The residuals you typically see on RR’s As-of view are the ones whose final zero-out transaction was a sales shipment, a cycle count, a cost revision, or any other non-ZBA path — JDE’s own write-off mechanism never fires for those, so the residual stays in the cardex history and surfaces in RR.

Step 1 — Confirm the as-of dates match

  1. Note the exact as-of date and time of the JDE Stock Valuation Report. If the report doesn’t print one, the as-of is “the moment it ran.”
  2. In RR, set the Inventory As-of view to the same as-of date. Use the period selector in the page header to pick the matching month / period.
  3. Re-run both. If the totals now match (or are very close), the original gap was just timing.
Tip

Same date isn’t always the same moment. If the JDE report ran at 9 AM and a large goods receipt posted at 10 AM, RR’s end-of-day As-of for that date will already include the receipt that the JDE 9 AM report doesn’t. Run both as close together as you can, ideally end-of-day after activity has settled.

Step 2 — Confirm both reports cover the same scope

If the totals are still off after aligning the dates, check that both reports are filtered to the same set of records:

  • Branch / plant — if JDE is filtered to one branch and RR is showing all branches, the totals won’t match.
  • Company / business unit — same idea. Confirm both reports are scoped to the same companies.
  • Item types — some Stock Valuation Report variants include or exclude certain item types (e.g., outside operations, kit components). Check the version specs on the JDE side.

Step 3 — If the dates and scope match, look at the variance sources

Once the inputs are aligned and the figures still differ, the gap is meaningful and worth investigating. Open the Inventory Reconciliation page in RR and check the variance source breakdown:

  • Cardex variance — the sum of F4111 cardex transactions doesn’t match the F41021 on-hand. Often a deleted cardex row or a direct F41021 adjustment without a matching F4111 entry. Whole-unit dollar gaps, not a few-cents residual.
  • Period placement — the date logic put a transaction in a different period than JDE’s GL date implies. Show up as case-level quantity gaps at period boundaries.
  • End-of-day — sales orders or work orders that haven’t settled yet.

Open the Inventory Reconciliation page and drill into the non-zero variance category to see which items contribute. If a single line is causing the gap, RR’s detail view will show it directly.

If you’re running a custom or self-generated report

The guidance above assumes you’re comparing RR to the standard JDE Stock Valuation Report. If your team is comparing RR to a custom report — a Smart Form, a UBE you wrote in-house, an Excel export with bespoke logic — we can’t comment on the gap without knowing the exact report definition: which tables it pulls from, how it filters, whether it rounds the same way, and where the as-of cut-off sits. The first step in those cases is to document the report’s logic, then match RR’s scope and dates to it the same way you would for the standard report.

When to escalate

Email rrsupport@getgsi.com if all of the following are true:

  • You’ve confirmed the JDE report and the RR view are pinned to the same as-of date and the same scope (branch / company / business unit).
  • The variance source breakdown doesn’t explain the gap.
  • The gap is material to the period close.

Calculated cost in RR doesn’t match the JDE item cost

RR shows a calculated cost for an item that’s different from the cost recorded on the JDE item record. This usually isn’t a bug — the two values are computed differently and represent different things.

What “calculated cost” means in RR

RR’s calculated cost is a derived value:

calculated cost = amount on hand ÷ quantity on hand

RR computes it because JDE has no cost history — the item record only carries the current cost. RR’s calculated cost gives you a reference figure derived from the actual ledger amounts and quantities at the period you’re viewing, which is usually what you want for reconciliation.

Why the JDE item cost can differ

  • Cost method — the JDE item record cost might be standard (07), weighted average (02), or actual (09), each updated on its own cadence. RR’s calculated cost is amount-divided-by-quantity at the snapshot, which doesn’t map cleanly to any single method.
  • Recent revaluation — if the JDE item cost was revalued recently (manual adjustment, weighted-average update, period roll), the post-revaluation cost won’t match the historical amount-on-hand ÷ quantity-on-hand calculation for prior periods.
  • Cost components — the JDE item cost may include components (overhead, freight, etc.) that aren’t in the inventory ledger amount RR is dividing by.

What to check

  1. Confirm which cost method JDE is using for the item (look at the cost component table or the cost ledger). The Product Costing Guide in RR University covers the variants.
  2. Compare the calculated cost not against today’s JDE item cost but against the JDE cost at the same as-of date. If the item was revalued recently, the historical cost matters more than the current one.
  3. If the item is part of a manufactured assembly, check whether outside operations or WIP cost components are skewing the on-hand amount RR is dividing by.
When the calculated cost is the right number

For period-close reconciliation against the GL, the calculated cost (amount ÷ quantity) is usually the right reference because it ties directly to the ledger amounts you’re reconciling. The JDE item record cost is the right reference for forward-looking pricing and standard-cost variance analysis — not for historical reconciliation.

When to escalate

Email rrsupport@getgsi.com if:

  • The calculated cost looks materially wrong for the item (e.g., it’s negative, zero with non-zero qty on hand, or off by orders of magnitude).
  • You’ve confirmed the cost method and the as-of-date alignment, and the difference still doesn’t make sense.

Cardex date logic — how RR assigns a transaction to a period

Every cardex transaction (F4111 row) ends up in exactly one fiscal period in RapidReconciler. The rule for picking that period is deterministic, but it isn’t always the JDE GL date you might expect — especially for transactions that haven’t posted to the GL yet, or that carry a future-dated GL. This page explains the rule and the two accounting methods that build on top of it.

The four-rule period date

For each cardex row, RR walks these rules in order and uses the first one that matches:

  1. GL Date exists and is in the future. If the cardex row has a GL date but it’s after today, RR uses today as the period date. (Future-dated GL entries are pulled back to the current period so they don’t silently park value in a future period.)
  2. GL Date exists. Otherwise, if there’s any GL date on the row, RR uses the GL date. This is the normal case for posted activity.
  3. No GL date and Creation Date is in the future. If the row hasn’t posted to the GL yet AND its creation date is after today, RR uses today. (Same rationale — no parking value in the future.)
  4. Use the Creation Date. Otherwise, RR uses the creation date as the period date. This is what happens for cardex activity that hasn’t been batched to the GL yet (work-order completions, sales-order shipments waiting on Sales Update, etc.).
Why a transaction may land in a different period than its JDE GL date

If a transaction’s GL date is in the future (e.g. a backdated reversal was entered with a forward-dated GL), rule 1 fires and RR places it in the current period — not the future period the GL would put it in. The transaction will move once it actually posts and the GL date catches up.

Why the rule looks the way it does

Each of the three candidate dates has a trade-off:

CandidateProCon
Transaction date (user-entered)Direct user inputAccuracy — depends on whoever entered the row
Creation date (system-generated)Always present, system-stampedGlobal “item update” events can shift it
GL dateTies RR to GL postingsDoesn’t reflect true inventory timing (date the goods actually moved)

RR’s four-rule order balances these — prefer the GL date when it’s available and reasonable, fall back to the creation date when the GL date isn’t there yet, and pull future-dated rows back to today so nothing parks ahead of the reporting cut-off.

Inventory Basis vs. GL Basis — the accounting method choice

The four-rule period date defines where the cardex row first lands. The accounting method defines what happens when its GL posting later arrives in a different period. There are two options:

Inventory Basis (default)

  • The transaction stays in the period it was originally placed in (per the four-rule logic).
  • When the GL posts later in a different period, the variance reclassifies from End of Day Variance to a Period Variance.
  • Prior period totals don’t shift around — once a period is closed, its cardex placement is sticky.

GL Basis

  • When the GL posts, the cardex transaction moves to the GL’s period.
  • No “Period Variance” classification — everything aligns to GL by construction.
  • Prior periods’ out-of-balance figures can change as late GL postings flow in.

Inventory Basis is the default and what most customers use because it preserves period totals once close is complete. GL Basis is occasionally chosen when the customer wants every cardex row to track the GL exactly, even if that means historical totals change retroactively. Confirm which method your instance is on before investigating a period-placement question — the right answer differs between the two.

Where this shows up when comparing to JDE

A typical mismatch caused by the date logic:

  • A work-order completion happened on the last day of Period 6 but hasn’t been batched to the GL yet.
  • JDE Stock Status, run on Period 7 Day 1, includes the on-hand impact of that completion because the quantity is already in the item-balance tables.
  • RR places the cardex row in Period 6 (rule 4, creation date), so the Period 7 Inventory As-of total in RR excludes it — the totals diverge by exactly that transaction’s extended value.
  • Once the work-order completion is batched and the GL posts, the row reclassifies (Inventory Basis: into a Period Variance staying in Period 6; GL Basis: moves to whatever period the GL date lands in).

When you’re reconciling a specific gap and the date logic looks like the cause, drill into the Inventory Reconciliation page’s variance breakdown for the period — End of Day Variance, Period Variance, and Transaction Variance each map to a different scenario in the rules above.

How to verify with the As Of Item Details export

When the symptom is a quantity-on-hand mismatch between RR and JDE for a specific period — e.g., an item shows 100 LB on the JDE cardex but 80 LB on RR’s As-of view for the same period end — the four-rule period logic is usually the cause. The fastest way to identify which transactions moved is the As Of Item Details export:

  1. From the Inventory module, open the Inventory As Of page for the period in question, drill into the item, and export the transaction list to Excel.
  2. Each row shows the cardex transaction’s period date as RR assigned it — this is the output of the four-rule logic, not the raw JDE GL date or transaction date.
  3. Pull the same item’s F4111 cardex from JDE for the same date range.
  4. Compare row by row. The transactions whose RR period date differs from the JDE GL date (or the JDE transaction date, where no GL date exists) are the ones the four-rule logic re-placed. Those are what shifted the As-of quantity.

Most of the time the gap comes from one or two transactions whose GL date hadn’t been written when RR processed them — RR fell back to rule 4 (creation date) and placed them in a period the user expected to land elsewhere. Once you can see which transactions moved, you can decide whether to wait for the next nightly job (which will reclassify them once the GL posts) or to investigate further.

Date logic vs. cardex variance

If the As Of Item Details export shows every RR period date matching the JDE GL / transaction date and the totals still differ, the cause isn’t the date logic — it’s a quantity-level disagreement between F4111 and F41021. See Cardex variance.

Cardex variance — when F4111 and F41021 disagree

Cardex variance is the quantity (and dollar) gap between two JDE tables for the same item:

  • F4111 — the Item Ledger, every cardex transaction (receipt, issue, transfer, adjustment, sale, completion).
  • F41021 — the Item Location Balances, the current quantity-on-hand summary by branch / lot / location.

For any item, summing all F4111 movements forward from the prior balance forward should equal the F41021 on-hand. When it doesn’t, the difference is cardex variance. This is structurally different from the rounding residuals (which are dollar-level only) and from the date logic (which is a period-placement question, not a quantity question).

Why a cardex variance happens

F4111 and F41021 are designed to stay in lock-step, but several real-world events break that:

  • Deleted F4111 row. Someone removed a cardex transaction in JDE without an offsetting adjustment. F41021 still reflects the post-transaction on-hand but F4111 no longer has the row that produced it.
  • Direct F41021 adjustment without an F4111 entry. A manual update to the on-hand balance (cycle count fix entered the wrong way, data-fix script, custom UBE) that didn’t write a corresponding cardex row.
  • Failed posting that updated one table but not the other. Most common around abnormally-terminated processes or partially-rolled-back custom workflows.
  • Custom programs that update F41021 directly. Some in-house extensions skip the standard P4112/P4113/P4114 paths and update F41021 in place — if they don’t also write F4111, every such update creates cardex variance.
  • Item Reclassifications (P4116) landing on a different GL account. Not a true variance, but it can look like one in account-level views: reclassifications write paired F4111 entries that move quantity and value between items or between item attributes (GL class, stocking type), and the From and To sides can resolve to different inventory accounts. See Item Reclassifications (P4116) in the cardex reference for the full pattern.

How it shows up in JDE-vs-RR comparison

The JDE Stock Valuation Report (R5541590) and the Stock Status pull quantity from the F41021 / item-branch tables. RR’s Inventory As-of view rebuilds the on-hand by summing F4111 forward from the balance forward. When F4111 and F41021 disagree, the two reports will too:

  • JDE > RR. F41021 has more on-hand than F4111 would account for — usually a deleted F4111 row, or a direct F41021 fix without a cardex entry.
  • RR > JDE. F4111 has more cumulative quantity than F41021 reflects — usually a missed or rolled-back F41021 update where the cardex row stayed.

The dollar gap on this kind of variance is typically a whole-unit-cost multiple (one or more cases times the lot cost), not the few-cents residual you’d see from rounding.

How to identify it

  1. Open the Inventory Reconciliation page in RR and look at the variance breakdown for the period.
  2. If you see a non-zero Cardex variance figure, that’s the symptom — F4111 and F41021 don’t agree for at least one item in the period.
  3. Click into the Cardex variance row to see which items contribute. RR lists each item with its F4111-summed quantity vs. its F41021 on-hand.
  4. For each contributing item, look at the F4111 history in JDE for unusual gaps (missing transaction numbers, deleted rows) or F41021 adjustments without a matching cardex row.

How RR handles it

RR doesn’t silently fix cardex variance — that would mask the underlying JDE data issue. Instead, it surfaces the variance on the Reconciliation page so the administrator can investigate and resolve it in JDE (typically by recording the missing F4111 row or backing out the rogue F41021 adjustment). Once F4111 and F41021 agree again, the cardex variance goes to zero on the next nightly refresh.

Don’t confuse cardex variance with the date logic

Both show up as “the totals don’t match” symptoms. The way to tell them apart: in the As Of Item Details export, if every transaction’s period date matches the JDE GL/transaction date but RR’s summed quantity is still different from JDE’s on-hand, you’re looking at cardex variance, not date logic. The opposite case — period dates don’t match but every cardex row is present on both sides — is date logic.

DMAAI Entry Integrity vs. JDE DMAAI’s analyzer

RapidReconciler’s DMAAI Entry Integrity report (Integrity Report 2) and the Export Analyzer’s JDE DMAAI’s template both look at JDE AAIs, but they look at different sets of AAIs. The counts won’t match, and that’s by design.

What each one covers

RR’s Integrity Report 2 is scoped to the AAIs RapidReconciler actually exercises during reconciliation — primarily the inventory-side AAIs that affect cardex / GL alignment for the items in RR’s reconciliation window. If RR doesn’t exercise an AAI, it won’t appear in the report. This keeps the output focused on what matters for RR-reported variances.

The JDE DMAAI’s analyzer reads the full F4095 export from JDE Database Browser (P964001). It sees every Distribution AAI across every JDE module — Inventory (41xx), Sales (42xx), Procurement (43xx), Intercompany (44xx) — whether or not RR ever touches them. It also looks for several anti-patterns that Integrity Report 2 doesn’t check: net-zero clearing pairs, transfer-clearing-account mismatches between 4220 and 4320, duplicate key tuples, and unrecognized AAI series.

Why the totals legitimately differ

The two reports are answering different questions:

  • Integrity Report 2 — “Are the AAIs RR uses set up correctly for the items in RR’s reconciliation scope?”
  • JDE DMAAI’s analyzer — “Is the customer’s entire F4095 table internally consistent, regardless of whether RR exercises every rule?”

A customer will often have far more findings in the JDE DMAAI’s analyzer than in Integrity Report 2 — not because RR is missing problems, but because the analyzer surfaces issues in modules (Sales, Procurement) RR doesn’t reconcile against. Those issues still affect JDE’s GL, just not RR’s reconciliation.

How to use them together

  • Start with Integrity Report 2 when reconciling an item-level cardex / GL variance in RR. The findings there map directly to what RR sees.
  • Use the JDE DMAAI’s analyzer when reviewing the broader JDE configuration — especially the transfer-clearing mismatch finding (AAI 4245 ST vs 4320 OT), which often explains hard-to-track Inventory-in-Transit residuals that don’t surface in Integrity Report 2 because they live on the Sales / Procurement sides.
  • Issues in Integrity Report 2 should usually also appear in the analyzer — if Integrity Report 2 flags an AAI as missing and the analyzer doesn’t, that’s a signal that the analyzer’s 4-tier hierarchy fallback (specific co + GL → wildcards → 00000 default) covered the case in production.
  • Open the analyzer’s Pattern Detail tab for the what / why / recommendation walkthrough on each flagged pattern. Each block has a hyperlinked evidence sub-table that jumps directly to the offending F4095 row on the source sheet.
  • Open the Transaction Tracing tab to see, by module and company, where each AAI in a transaction’s journal entry resolves through the F4095 hierarchy. Useful when investigating why a posting landed on an unexpected account — the tracing tab tells you which GL classes share the resolved account and which fall back to the wildcard.

When to escalate

If a customer has a high-priority finding (P1) in the JDE DMAAI’s analyzer that they can’t explain — especially a net-zero pair or a transfer-clearing mismatch — bring it to their JDE administrator with the analyzer output attached. Both findings frequently explain stubborn reconciliation residuals that no other report surfaces. rrsupport@getgsi.com can help interpret the output if needed.

Email RR Support

If the steps in the topics above didn’t resolve the discrepancy, email rrsupport@getgsi.com. The more context you include, the faster we can help.

What to include

To investigate efficiently we need enough detail to reproduce the gap and confirm which variance source is responsible. The minimum useful submission is one fully-worked example item; if you’re seeing more than one type of variance, include one example per type.

  • Both source reports — the JDE Stock Valuation Report (or whichever JDE report you ran) and the RR view you’re comparing it to. PDF or Excel for both.
  • The as-of date and time for each report.
  • The scope — which branches, companies, and business units each report covered.
  • What you tried — which of the steps above you walked (date alignment, scope check, variance source breakdown) and what each one told you.
  • The gap — the actual dollar / quantity difference, and whether it appears across many items or is concentrated in a few.

Per-item example (the most important attachment)

Pick at least one item that exhibits the gap and send everything for that one item:

  • Item number and branch / plant.
  • What the JDE report shows for the item — quantity, amount, and the lot / location breakdown if it’s a lot-managed item.
  • What RR shows for the same item, same period — the QOH and amount from the Inventory As-of view.
  • JDE cardex (F4111) export for the item over the same date range — every transaction row, ordered by GL date or transaction date.
  • RR As Of Item Details export for the item, same period — every row with the period date RR assigned.

The two transaction lists side-by-side are what tell us whether the cause is rounding residuals, cardex date logic, cardex variance, or something else entirely. Without them we’re guessing.

Multiple variance types — one example each

If your audit turned up more than one pattern (e.g., a batch of items with zero-qty residuals AND a couple of items with case-level quantity gaps), pick one example item per pattern and send the full per-item package for each. That way each pattern can be confirmed independently rather than triaged from a single mixed example. Typical patterns and what each example helps us confirm:

  • Rounding residuals — an item with QOH = 0 and a small dollar residual (e.g., ±$0.01 to a few dollars). The cardex + As-of exports for this one item show the summed pre-rounded amounts that produced the residual.
  • Cardex date logic — an item with a whole-case quantity gap at the period boundary. The two exports will show one or more transactions whose RR period date differs from the JDE GL or transaction date.
  • Cardex variance — an item where every cardex row in JDE appears with the same date in RR, but the summed quantity still doesn’t match the F41021 on-hand. Indicates an F4111 vs F41021 disagreement we’ll need to track to its source in JDE.
