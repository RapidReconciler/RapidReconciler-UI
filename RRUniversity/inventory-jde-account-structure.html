JD Edwards Account Structure — Reference

Every General Ledger account in JD Edwards is three segments joined by dots: a Business Unit (where), an Object Account (what), and an optional Subsidiary (extra detail). Once you can read those three segments at a glance, every account number in a Transaction Detail, a DMAAI report, or an analyzer's HOW card stops being a string and starts being a story.

Overview

JDE General Ledger accounts have a fixed, three-segment structure. Every account in the chart, every DMAAI entry, every cardex row, every F0911 posting carries the same shape. The segments answer three questions in order: where did the activity happen, what kind of account is it, and (optionally) which sub-detail within that account.

The Three Segments

BusinessUnit.ObjectAccount.Subsidiary
BU — where OBJ — what SUB — detail (optional)

BUBusiness Unit — where the activity occurred

The Business Unit answers where the financial activity took place. It typically maps to one of:

  • Company — the legal-entity rollup
  • Cost center — a sub-entity used for cost tracking
  • Department — a functional grouping within a company
  • Location — a physical site, branch, or plant

BU values can be numeric or alphanumeric. Examples:

  • 100 — a numeric BU (often a small / single-site setup)
  • 5000000 — a longer numeric BU (common in larger multi-branch shops)
  • MFG01 — an alphanumeric BU naming a manufacturing site

OBJObject Account — what type of account

The Object Account is the natural account — the GL line item type. It tells you what the activity is, independent of where it occurred. Object accounts follow typical accounting-ledger ranges:

RangeType
1000–1999Assets — cash, receivables, inventory, fixed assets
2000–2999Liabilities — payables, accruals, debt
3000–3999Equity (commonly) — owner's equity, retained earnings
4000–4999Revenue — sales, service income
5000–5999Expenses — COGS, operating expenses, payroll

The ranges above are conventions, not enforced rules. Customer charts of accounts may drift — some shops use 6000–6999 for expenses, others use 3000s for COGS rather than equity. The principle stands: the Object Account is the question what, and its numeric value usually signals the category.

Why this matters for reconciliation: when the analyzer says “the GL posted to 5000.140100.RM1” you can read it without looking anything up. The OBJ 140100 is in the 1xxxxx range, so it's an Asset — specifically an inventory-related account given the typical 14xxxx convention. The BU 5000 identifies the branch / plant. The SUB RM1 is the extra detail layer (probably a product-line or cost-tracking tag for that specific inventory slice).

SUBSubsidiary — extra detail within the object

The Subsidiary is optional but very common. When present, it splits a single object account into finer-grained slices for tracking purposes. Typical uses:

  • Product line — split inventory or revenue by category (1000.4000.MTG = mortgage revenue, 1000.4000.LIFE = life-insurance revenue)
  • Project — when project accounting tracks costs against a specific job (100.5100.PRJ-042)
  • Customer / vendor detail — A/R or A/P sliced by specific counterparty
  • Cost tracking — for management reporting that needs sub-account granularity (5000.140100.RM1 — the RM1 slice of raw-material inventory at branch 5000)

Many accounts have no Subsidiary at all. When the third segment is blank, the account number is just BU.OBJ. The dot still separates the segments visually but trailing-empty SUBs are usually rendered without the trailing dot.

Reading Accounts in the Wild

Once you know the segments, you can decode any JDE account at a glance:

AccountRead it as
100.5100.MKTGBU 100 (a small-shop company / cost center), OBJ 5100 (an expense in the 5xxx range), SUB MKTG (marketing slice).
5000.140000BU 5000 (a multi-branch shop's plant code), OBJ 140000 (an asset in the 14xxxx series — typically raw-material inventory). No SUB.
5000.140100.RM1Same BU, OBJ 140100 (a separate inventory asset slot from 140000), SUB RM1 (a product-line tracking code).
9000.501000.10BU 9000 (a different branch / company prefix), OBJ 501000 (a COGS / sales-cost expense), SUB 10 (numeric sub-tracking).
MFG01.4220BU MFG01 (alphanumeric manufacturing site), OBJ 4220 (revenue / sales-side account). No SUB.

Looking Up Accounts in DMAAI Tables

The Distribution / Manufacturing AAI tables (DMAAIs, stored in JDE as F4095) don't store the assembled account string. Instead, they store each segment as its own column, and JDE assembles them at posting time. When you look at an F4095 row — either in JDE Database Browser, in RR's Integrity Report 0 (JDE DMAAIs), or in a previously-analyzed JDE DMAAI's workbook — the columns are:

F4095 columnWhat it carries
Business Unit / MCUThe BU segment of the resolved account (when not flex)
Obj Acct / OBJThe OBJ segment
SubThe SUB segment (often blank)
BU Flex / SubFlexFlexible Accounting flags — when on, JDE constructs the BU or SUB at posting time from transaction fields rather than reading it directly from the AAI. See the DMAAI reference for how Flex affects routing.

To find the GL account a transaction posted through, you take its (company, AAI table, order type, doc type, GL class, cost type) routing key, look it up in F4095 via the four-tier hierarchy (specific co + specific GL class → specific co + wildcard → 00000 default + specific GL class → 00000 default + wildcard), and assemble the resolved account from the matching row's BU + OBJ + SUB columns.

RapidReconciler's analyzer does this lookup automatically when a JDE DMAAI's workbook is preloaded on the Export Analyzer's Preload card. The analyzer's Pattern 5.4 (Account Mismatch) HOW card uses the lookup result to compare the cardex's expected account against the GL's actual posted account — and tells you which of the two real causes (DMAAI changed post-transaction, or DMAAI overridden at posting time) is more likely based on whether the misposted account appears anywhere else in the F4095.
