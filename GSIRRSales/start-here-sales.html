Part 1 · Process Tour

The sales-to-install workflow at a glance

Every RapidReconciler customer follows the same eight steps from first touch to going live. You own seven of those eight; the last one is the customer triggering the technical install. Read through the steps below, then come back to the document tour for the artifacts you send at each step.

The flowchart below shows the full picture at a glance. Four swim lanes divide the work by ownership: Sales (you), Customer I/T (the customer’s technical staff), RR DBA (a GSI tech-team member who joins one of your meetings and takes over after handoff), and RR Network Tech (a GSI tech-team member who participates in PoC preliminary installs alongside the RR DBA). Click any numbered block with a coloured border to jump to that document’s entry below.

The two principles to internalise

  • You don’t do the technical install. Your job ends when the customer emails RR Support to trigger the install. The RR DBA picks it up from there.
  • Only raise a PoC when necessary. The default path skips PoC entirely. PoCs are a real cost (billable GSI time on the customer’s servers) and should only be raised when a prospect needs to see RR running against their own data before signing.
Step 1

Identify prospect

Sales

The opening move. Outreach, qualification, first conversations — the work of getting a JD Edwards customer interested enough to want to learn more about RapidReconciler.

What you do: first-touch outreach using the Overview and Fact Sheet for context, optionally pointing prospects at the Self-Guided Tour for asynchronous qualification.

No documents sent yet. Collateral pieces (Overview, Fact Sheet, Self-Guided Tour) are tools for this stage but don’t map to a flowchart artifact.

Step 2

PoC needed? (decision)

Sales

A judgement call. No is the default and proceeds to Step 3 (send provisioning). Yes peels off into the optional PoC side path (Steps 2A and 2B), then rejoins the main flow.

How to decide: raise a PoC only when a prospect explicitly needs to see RapidReconciler running against their own JDE data before they’ll sign. Most customers don’t need this — the Self-Guided Tour and a discovery conversation are usually sufficient. If you’re unsure, default to No and see if the prospect asks for a PoC themselves.

Step 2A

Send preliminary PoC agreement

Sales

(Optional — only if Step 2 = Yes.)

What you do: send the customer a Proof of Concept agreement covering scope, billable GSI time, and duration. The PoC is a paid engagement; the agreement makes the cost and limits explicit before any technical work starts.

Document: Proof of Concept.

Step 2B

PoC engagement — preliminary install

Customer I/T + RR DBA (shared)

(Optional — only if Step 2 = Yes.)

This step is not your work. The RR DBA runs a preliminary install on the customer’s servers; Customer I/T provides access. You’re facilitating, not executing.

Your part: coordinate the calendar, manage the commercial relationship through the engagement, and stay in the loop on outcomes. When the PoC completes, you rejoin the main flow at Step 3.

Step 3

Send provisioning document

Sales

What you do: send the customer the Provisioning Document. This is a customer-facing technical-requirements pack covering deployment topology, server specs, JDE source connectivity, software & licensing, network requirements, and security posture.

Why this comes before commercial close: the customer needs to review it with their I/T, security, and JDE teams. A “yes” on commercials is hollow if their I/T then blocks on a security review. Sending Provisioning early surfaces those reviews before contracts.

Document: Provisioning Document.

Step 4

Purchase agreement? (decision)

Sales

The commercial decision. Yes proceeds to Step 5 (signed contract). No ends the process — the prospect doesn’t become a customer.

What you do: run the negotiation, address objections, agree on number of companies licensed, license dates, pricing.

Step 5

Generate & send signed contract

Sales

What you do: generate the contract from the Contract Template, send it to the customer for signature, and log the executed copy.

What goes in the contract: number of companies purchased (drives the Maximum Companies Allowed value the tech team configures in VALC), exact 5-digit JDE company numbers to license, license start and end dates. These are the values RR Network Tech executes against later in the install — the contract is the source of truth.

Document: Contract Template.

Step 6

Arrange discovery call

Sales (with RR DBA joining)

What you do: schedule the joint kickoff between Sales (you), Customer I/T, and RR DBA. This is the technical handoff — the meeting where your customer relationship transitions from “commercial” to “technical install”. You’re still on it; you facilitate; but the substantive content is between Customer I/T and RR DBA.

Set expectations with the customer: the technical install is a 2-hour live web meeting where Customer I/T must be available the full duration. Then a separate VALC working session with RR Network Tech for the agent install. Surfacing this during your commercial conversation prevents scheduling friction at handoff.

Document: Discovery Call.

Step 7

Send installation prep document

Sales

What you do: after the discovery call, send Customer I/T the Installation Prep Document. This is the checklist they execute against to ready their environment.

Why this is the last thing you send: the Installation Prep Document ends with an email handoff — Customer I/T emails rrsupport@getgsi.com when their environment is ready, which triggers Step 8 and starts the technical workflow on the GSI tech side. From this point, the install is in the tech team’s hands.

Document: Installation Prep Document.

Step 8

Customer triggers technical install

Customer I/T

Not your work, but the moment your work pays off. Customer I/T completes the install-prep checklist and emails rrsupport@getgsi.com. RR Support routes that email to RR DBA to start the database-side install; RR DBA hands off to RR Network Tech for VALC; the customer goes live.

What happens after handoff (so you can answer customer questions): the database side takes a 2-hour live web meeting (RR DBA + Customer I/T together). After that completes and the initial data load is verified, RR Network Tech picks up VALC, installs the agent on the customer’s application server, configures licensing, and sends a final handoff email to the customer with login credentials. End-to-end depends on customer I/T availability but is typically days, not weeks.

Part 2 · Document Tour

Every document you’re responsible for

Eight documents in total: five process documents (the artifacts that move the workflow forward) and three pieces of collateral (the outreach materials you use during prospecting). Read these in roughly the order shown — the process docs are presented in the order you send them, and the collateral after.

Draft — manager to validate

The “when to read it” guidance below assumes a typical onboarding sequence. Your manager may have a different recommended order based on the prospects you’ll work with first — check before treating these as authoritative.

Proof of Concept

Step 2A Open document →

The optional PoC agreement template. Covers when to propose a PoC, scope of the preliminary install (always run on the customer’s servers), billable GSI time, the preliminary agreement template, and conversion to provisioning if the PoC succeeds.

When you’ll use it: only when a prospect needs to see RapidReconciler running against their own JDE data before they’ll sign. The doc itself emphasises that PoCs should only be raised when necessary — not every prospect needs one.

Provisioning Document

Step 3 Open document →

The customer-facing technical requirements pack. Covers deployment topology, server specs, JD Edwards source-platform connectivity, software & licensing breakdown, network requirements, and security posture.

When you send it: before commercial close. The customer reviews it with their I/T, security, and JDE teams. Sending it early surfaces internal blockers (security reviews, change-control queues) before they bite a signed contract.

Contract Template

Step 5 Internal only

The Master Software License Agreement and Order Form. This template is not published in the public knowledge base — it is maintained internally and provided to authorized sales staff through GSI's internal channels. Browser-fillable: type the customer’s information into the highlighted fields, then save the page as a PDF and upload to DocuSign. Signature fields are tagged so DocuSign places them automatically.

When you send it: after Yes on the Step 4 commercial decision. The signed contract is the source of truth for number of companies purchased, exact 5-digit JDE company numbers, and license start/end dates — the values the tech team uses when configuring VALC.

Discovery Call

Step 6 Open document →

The agenda and participant list for the joint kickoff between Sales, Customer I/T, and RR DBA. Includes pre-call prep, technical handoff structure, and what should be covered — company count, JDE company numbers, maintenance window, go-live date, additional databases (test/dev/train), password policy preferences, and the customer-side RapidReconciler administrator nomination.

When you use it: before scheduling the discovery call (to brief yourself on the agenda) and during the call (to keep the conversation on track). Send the agenda to attendees with the meeting invite.

Installation Prep Document

Step 7 Open document →

Walks Customer I/T through the pre-install steps: topology selection, source-platform identification, server provisioning, Windows security, the pre-install checklist. Ends with the email handoff to rrsupport@getgsi.com that triggers the technical install.

When you send it: after the discovery call. This is the last document you send — once Customer I/T works through it and emails RR Support, the install is on the tech team’s plate.

Collateral — outreach & qualification

Three pieces designed for the prospecting stage (Step 1). Use them alongside the process documents above to move a prospect from first conversation through to discovery call.

Self-Guided Tour

Collateral Open document →

An interactive walk-through of RapidReconciler that prospects can run on their own time, without a live demo. Click-through screens covering the main features.

When to send: after first contact, to qualify interest before booking a discovery call. Particularly useful when the prospect is interested but their calendar is tight — they can run through the tour async and come back with informed questions.

Overview

Collateral Open document →

High-level product overview suitable for first-touch outreach and executive-level conversations. Covers what RapidReconciler does, who it’s for, and the key business outcomes — inventory reconciliation against JDE, in-transit, RNV, cardex, period-end close pain.

When to use: first conversations, executive-level pitches, the “what is this thing” question. Read it cover to cover early in your onboarding — it’s the elevator pitch you’ll repeat in your own words on every prospect call.

Fact Sheet

Collateral Open document →

One-page reference with feature list, supported environments, integration points, and licensing tiers. The technical companion to the Overview.

When to use: when a technical evaluator or procurement team asks for specifics. Pairs well with the Overview — Overview is for the executive sponsor, Fact Sheet is for the team they hand it to.
Wrap-up · Hands-on

Try it: build a practice contract

Before you send a real one, run through the contract template once with a fictional customer. The exercise takes about ten minutes. It walks you through the workflow you’ll use on every deal — browser fill, generate the PDF, ready it for DocuSign — without any chance of accidentally sending a draft to a real prospect.

The fictional customer for this exercise is Acme Foods Inc., an Ohio corporation. Round numbers below; nothing here corresponds to any real customer.

Step 1 · Open the template

Open the contract template in a new tab. Your work is auto-saved to the browser as you type, so it’s safe to switch back and forth between this guide and the template.

Step 3 · Notice these things while you fill it in

The template is built to make sales work fast. You won’t use every feature consciously, but knowing they’re there means you’ll lean on them naturally:

Did you notice…
  • The Total field filled itself when you entered License Fee and Maintenance Fee. It’s an editable field if you ever need to override it (e.g., a custom credit), but the math is done for you by default.
  • Typing the Effective Date once filled it in four places in the document — the master agreement subhead, the master agreement opening paragraph, the order form subhead, and the order form footer reference. Same for Licensee Legal Name, which appears in five places including all three signature blocks.
  • The Print / Save as PDF button stayed greyed out until every field was filled. Try clicking it before you’re done — you’ll get a list of what’s missing and the page will scroll to the first empty field.
  • The field counter at the top of the template shows progress. Useful sanity check before you commit to a print.

Step 4 · Save the PDF

Click Print / Save as PDF. In the browser’s print dialog, choose Save as PDF as the destination (not Microsoft Print to PDF). The suggested filename will be RapidReconciler-Master-Agreement-and-Order-Form.pdf. Save it somewhere you can find it — your Downloads folder is fine for practice.

Open the saved PDF. You should see:

  • The GSI and RapidReconciler logos in the header on every page
  • 11 pages total — 9 pages of the master agreement, then 2 pages of the order form
  • Every value you entered, with no yellow placeholder highlights, no toolbar, no template notice
  • An order table on page 10 showing Total: USD $24,000
  • Empty signature lines on page 9 and page 11 — DocuSign will fill these

If the PDF doesn’t look right (wrong totals, missing values, weird formatting) come back to the template tab and check what didn’t propagate. The template is auto-saved, so re-opening it after a bad print won’t lose your work — the print itself is what clears the saved data.

Step 5 · Show your manager

Manager check-in

Before you do this on a real customer, send your practice PDF to your manager and walk through it together. They’ll know the local conventions for things this template can’t enforce — preferred date formats, how to phrase the Licensee Legal Name (with or without “Inc.”), what the consulting rate should be in your region, and whether your account uses anchor-tag DocuSign sends or manual field placement.

This is also the right moment to ask any questions about the contract clauses themselves. The template handles the mechanical stuff; your manager handles the judgement calls.

Optional · Try it again with your own values

Once you’ve done the Acme Foods walk-through, run it once more with a different fictional customer and different numbers — whatever feels realistic for your region. The point is to make the workflow muscle memory before your first real prospect lands.

Wrap-up

What to do next

You’ve walked the process, met the documents, and built a practice contract. Three more suggested steps:

  1. Read the Overview cover to cover. It’s the shortest document and the one you’ll quote from most. The pitch lives there.
  2. Skim the Provisioning Document and Installation Prep Document. You won’t fill these in — the customer does — but knowing what’s in them lets you answer the early objections (“how complicated is the install?”) without escalating to the tech team.
  3. Re-read the flowchart at the top of this page alongside the eight step cards. Seeing the diagram and reading the prose at the same time is the fastest way to internalise the swim lanes.
