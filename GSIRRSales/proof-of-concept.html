Proof of Concept

When to propose one, what it includes, what it costs, and how to get a preliminary agreement signed.

A Proof of Concept is an optional, billable, preliminary install of RapidReconciler on the customer's own servers. It exists to de-risk a purchase decision when a prospect can't commit without first seeing the product run against their own data. It is not the default sales path — raise it only when necessary.

Default behavior

Sales does not proactively offer a PoC. The standard path is provisioning → purchase agreement → signed contract → install. A PoC adds time and billable cost on both sides — only suggest it when a prospect's specific objection genuinely requires it.

When to propose a PoC

Use these signals to decide whether the conversation has actually reached "PoC needed?" or whether you can keep moving toward a Purchase Agreement on the standard path.

PoC may be appropriate when…
  • The prospect has a complex or unusual JDE configuration that they need to see RapidReconciler handle before committing.
  • Internal sign-off requires a working demonstration on the prospect's own data, not a generic demo.
  • An incumbent solution is in place and the customer needs evidence of equivalence or improvement.
  • The prospect has explicitly asked for a trial or pilot and a discovery call alone won't satisfy the request.
Skip the PoC when…
  • The prospect is asking for a PoC out of habit or curiosity rather than a specific blocker.
  • A standard discovery call and demo would resolve the same questions.
  • The customer is not prepared to cover the billable GSI time.
  • The customer's environment isn't ready to host a preliminary install (no server access, no JDE connectivity, etc.).
  • Procurement timeline is short enough that the PoC would push the close past a relevant deadline.
Sales judgment

If you're unsure, talk to the RR DBA before raising the PoC option with the customer. A short internal conversation often reveals whether the customer's questions can be answered another way.

Scope of the engagement

A PoC is a preliminary install — not a full production deployment, and not a sandbox in GSI's environment.

What's in scope

  • Installation on the customer's servers. RR DBA leads the install. Customer I/T provides server access, network, and JDE connection details.
  • Connection to the customer's JDE source data. Either production or a representative copy — the customer chooses.
  • Initial data load. Enough to demonstrate the product against the customer's own records.
  • A working demo session. Customer-facing walk-through of RapidReconciler running on their own data.
  • A defined endpoint. The PoC has a fixed duration agreed in the preliminary agreement — typically a small number of weeks. After that, the engagement ends or rolls forward into the standard purchase path.

What's out of scope

  • Ongoing support, training, or production-grade SLAs.
  • Custom development, custom reports, or non-standard integrations.
  • Multi-environment installs (e.g., separate dev/test/prod) — the PoC is a single environment.
  • Production data migration. The PoC's data load is for demonstration only and is not a migration.
Always on customer infrastructure

The PoC always runs on the customer's servers. GSI does not host PoC environments — the value of the PoC is specifically that the customer sees the product running in their own environment, against their own data.

Cost & billable time

The PoC carries a charge to cover GSI time — primarily RR DBA hours for the preliminary install and demo, plus a small amount of Sales coordination time. This is not a free trial. Be direct about this with the customer when the PoC is first raised.

Pricing — needs to be filled in

Specific PoC pricing, billing cadence, and payment terms have not yet been finalized in this document. Coordinate with management to confirm the current rate sheet before sending a preliminary agreement to a customer.

What customers should expect to be billed for

  • RR DBA install hours (preliminary database setup, SSIS package configuration, initial data load).
  • Demo session preparation and delivery.
  • Sales coordination during the PoC window.
  • Any agreed scope expansion that comes up mid-PoC, billed at the same rate.

What customers do not pay separately for

  • The preliminary agreement itself (this is a sales artifact, not a billable deliverable).
  • The discovery call that follows the PoC if they convert — that's part of the standard path.

Preliminary agreement

Before any RR DBA time is booked, the customer signs a preliminary agreement covering scope, duration, and cost. This is a Sales action and corresponds to Step 2A in the workflow.

What the preliminary agreement covers

SectionPurpose
ScopeWhat's in and out (mirrors the “In scope / Out of scope” sections above, but specific to this engagement).
DurationStart date, end date, and what triggers an extension.
Customer responsibilitiesServer access, JDE connection details, named technical contact, sign-off authority for the demo session.
Cost & billing termsRate, estimated total, billing cadence, payment terms.
Outcome pathWhat happens at the end of the PoC — either the engagement winds down, or the customer continues into the standard purchase path. Not a commitment to purchase.
TerminationHow either side ends the engagement early and what's owed.

The conversation that comes before

Before sending a preliminary agreement, Sales has typically already had a short internal discussion about whether a PoC is warranted (see When to propose a PoC). Once that's settled, the agreement formalizes what was discussed verbally with the customer:

  1. Confirm the customer understands the PoC is billable and not free.
  2. Confirm the customer can host a preliminary install (server, network, JDE access).
  3. Agree on a duration window.
  4. Send the preliminary agreement for signature.
  5. Once signed, schedule the install with RR DBA — this is Step 2B.

What happens after the PoC

The PoC does not replace the standard sales path. After the PoC concludes, the customer either ends the engagement or continues into the main flow at Step 3 — Send provisioning document. The provisioning document, purchase agreement, signed contract, discovery call, and final installation all still happen.

Why it rejoins at Step 3

The PoC install is preliminary — not the production deployment. Even when a customer has seen the product running on their own data, the formal sales process (provisioning requirements, purchase agreement, signed contract, discovery call) still needs to happen before the production install. The PoC is evidence the customer needs to commit; the rest of the path is what makes that commitment real.

If the customer converts

  • The PoC engagement ends per the preliminary agreement.
  • The customer enters the main path at Step 3 (Provisioning Document).
  • RR DBA may reuse some of the PoC environment knowledge during the eventual production install — coordinate with them so the customer isn't asked the same questions twice.

If the customer does not convert

  • The PoC engagement ends per the preliminary agreement.
  • Final billing closes out for the agreed PoC scope.
  • RR DBA decommissions any GSI-installed components from the customer's environment per the agreement.

Common questions

Can the PoC run in GSI's environment instead of the customer's?

No. The PoC always runs on the customer's servers, against the customer's data. That's what makes it a PoC and not a generic demo.

Can we waive the cost for a strategic prospect?

Pricing decisions sit with management, not Sales. Don't promise a free or discounted PoC in conversation — route the question to management first.

What if the customer wants the PoC to last longer than originally agreed?

Extensions are possible but require an amendment to the preliminary agreement, and they're billed at the same rate. Don't let an extension happen informally — document it.

Does a successful PoC mean the customer is committed to buy?

No. The preliminary agreement explicitly does not commit the customer to a purchase. The PoC outcome path is documented but non-binding — the customer can walk away.

Can the PoC count toward the eventual production install?

The PoC's environment, scope, and data load are not designed to roll into production. Treat the production install as a fresh engagement that follows the standard path. RR DBA may reuse customer-environment knowledge gained during the PoC, but the install itself is new.

Who from GSI is the customer-facing technical contact during the PoC?

RR DBA. Sales remains the relationship owner and handles the agreement, billing, and conversion conversation. RR DBA owns the technical conversation throughout the install and demo.
