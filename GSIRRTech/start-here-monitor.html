Part 1 · Mailbox Tour

What the mailbox is — and what it isn’t

rrsupport@getgsi.com is the single inbound address listed across every GSI RapidReconciler document — the one customers, sales, and the tech team all write to. As Helpdesk Tech, you read every inbound message, identify what it is, and either resolve it yourself at Tier 1 or route it to the right person on the tech team. You don’t do the deeper technical work of installs, migrations, or schema fixes — that’s what RR DBA, RR Network Tech, and RR Developer are for.

What the mailbox is

  • The single trigger for the tech-side install. Customer I/T submits their pre-install email here; that email is what kicks off RR DBA's database install.
  • The seam between the database install and the VALC install. RR DBA sends a database-ready notification here when their work is done; that email is what kicks off RR Network Tech's VALC install.
  • The customer’s primary contact point for change requests, password-policy activations, license renewals, and ad-hoc support questions across every live customer.
  • The KB’s feedback channel. Every documentation page ends with a feedback band pointing here for corrections.

What the mailbox isn’t

  • It isn’t a ticketing system. Treat it as a triage queue: you read, you classify, you forward, you note what you did. If something needs structured tracking, push that through whatever your team uses for tickets — the mailbox itself isn’t it.
  • It isn’t where deeper technical work gets done. You’ll resolve a portion of inbound at Tier 1 directly from the mailbox. Anything beyond Tier 1 — database changes, VALC-side configuration, schema or product-code issues — gets routed to RR DBA, RR Network Tech, or RR Developer, and your involvement on that thread is complete unless they bounce it back to you.
  • It isn’t a place to make decisions outside Tier 1 scope. If you’re unsure whether something is a database issue or a VALC issue and it’s past what Tier 1 covers, route it where the symptoms point and let the receiving tech reroute if needed. Don’t try to diagnose deeper than Tier 1 supports.
Draft — manager to validate

This guide doesn’t cover specifics like response-time SLAs, ticketing-tool integration, shift handoff procedures, or out-of-office coverage. Confirm those with your manager before your first shift — they’re team-specific and not in the source documentation.

Who sends what to this mailbox

Five sources put email into rrsupport@getgsi.com. Recognising the source quickly is half the job — the other half is recognising the message type within that source.

  • Customer I/T — the customer's own technical staff. They send the pre-install submission that triggers a new install, plus password-policy activation requests and ad-hoc change requests over a customer’s lifetime.
  • RR DBA — a GSI tech-team member. Sends one specific email, the database-ready notification, that hands a customer off to VALC. Every install produces exactly one of these.
  • Sales — usually only when a renewal or scope change has been agreed and they’re looping in the tech team to update VALC.
  • End users at customer companies — occasionally. Most user-management questions should go to the customer’s own RapidReconciler administrator; if they reach this mailbox, you’ll usually redirect.
  • Anyone reading the KB — documentation-feedback emails come from the feedback band on every page.

Customer support emails — how to handle them

A meaningful share of inbound is generic customer support: a user reports something isn’t working, sends a screenshot of an error, asks why their data looks off. For these, the entry point is the Troubleshooting Hub’s email-lookup pane. Paste the customer’s message in, get matching scenario cards, walk the customer through the steps, and either resolve at Tier 1 or escalate cleanly — without losing the customer’s email content along the way.

Open the Troubleshooting Hub with email lookup ready

Opens in a new tab. The email-lookup pane is auto-expanded; the textarea is focused so a paste lands there immediately.

The six-step flow

  1. Read the email once for the gist. Identify whether it’s a customer-support question (you’re using this section) or one of the structured types in the routing playbook below. If it has a recognisable subject line like RapidReconciler Pre-Install Submission or Database Ready, route it via the playbook instead.
  2. Open the hub with email lookup ready using the button above (or navigate to the Troubleshooting Hub manually and click the green Start from a customer email bar).
  3. Paste the customer’s email into the textarea and click Find matching cards. The hub shows up to three candidate scenarios, ranked Strong / Possible / Weak match, with the matched words highlighted in the symptom previews.
  4. Pick the best-matching card. Read the symptom and likely-causes sections to confirm it fits, then walk the customer through the troubleshooting steps. Most Tier 1 cards resolve here.
  5. If resolved: reply to the customer with what you found and what they did. Note the resolution in your team’s ticket system if you use one separately. Done.
  6. If not resolved after the steps on the card: escalate via one of the two paths in the next section.

Two escalation paths — pick one

When a Tier 1 attempt doesn’t resolve the issue, the troubleshooting hub gives you two ways to hand off to the next tier (Network Tech for most things). They’re both valid — the right pick depends on how formal the situation is.

  • Reply-all on the customer’s email with the prose handoff prefilled by the Copy escalation summary button on the scenario card. Use this when the conversation is fast-moving, the customer is on the phone, or you want everything to stay in one email thread. Includes To / From / Subject, the customer details, the steps you tried, and a one-line note on why you’re escalating. Quick and informal.
  • Create a JSM ticket via the blue Create JSM ticket button on the scenario card. Use this when the issue needs formal tracking, won’t resolve in your shift, or has SLA implications. The ticket lands in the RapidReconciler Support project as a Support issue. The troubleshooting context is auto-filled in the description; if you’d previously pasted the customer’s email into the lookup pane, that’s carried into the description automatically (or onto your clipboard, for very long emails).
Default to JSM when in doubt

If you’re unsure which path to use, default to JSM. A formally-tracked ticket that turns out to have been simple is cheap; an email handoff that gets lost in someone’s inbox is expensive. Reply-all is for cases where you’re actively chatting with the customer and the next-tier tech can drop into the same thread.

What you’ll see when you click Create JSM ticket

Jira opens the Create Issue dialog in a new tab with most fields prefilled from the scenario context. Here’s what each field is for:

Project
RapidReconciler Support — already set, don’t change.
Issue Type
Support — already set. The neutral starting bucket; can be reclassified to Bug later if it turns out to be a defect.
Summary
Pre-filled with [GSI RR] {scenario title}. Edit the part after [GSI RR] if you want a more specific one-liner — the team will see this in the queue.
Attachment
Drop zone. If the customer’s email had screenshots or files, drop them here. Pasting the email into the lookup pane only captures text; attachments don’t come through the clipboard, so save them locally first and drag them in here after the dialog opens.
Due date
Leave blank unless the issue has a hard deadline (e.g. customer cutover scheduled for a specific date). Most support tickets don’t need this.
Description
Pre-filled with the troubleshooting context: scenario name, symptom, the numbered steps from the card, plus a reminder to attach the customer’s email. If you’d pasted the email into the lookup pane, it’s already inserted in the description. Review and tick off which steps you ran successfully versus which were inconclusive.
Assignee
Defaults to Automatic (project routing rules decide). Leave it on Automatic unless you have a specific reason to assign manually. Don’t click Assign to me — you’re escalating, not taking ownership.

Reference screenshot: jsm-create-dialog.png. The exact field set may evolve if the team adds custom fields; this list reflects the current minimal form.

Click Create when you’re done. The new ticket gets an ID like RS-123, lands in the team’s queue, and the assigned tech gets notified through normal Jira channels. Your involvement is complete unless they bounce it back to you.

Attachments reminder

The page can’t move screenshots or files for you — the clipboard doesn’t carry attachments. The workflow is: save attachments to your machine first, paste the email body into the lookup pane, click Create JSM ticket, then drag the attachment files from your machine into the Jira dialog’s Attachment drop zone before clicking Create.

Three principles to internalise

  • Read the subject first. The two structurally important emails — the pre-install submission and the database-ready notification — have predictable subject-line formats. Recognising those lets you route in seconds. The full subject patterns are in the routing playbook below.
  • Route on signal, not on guess. If the subject and body clearly identify the type, route confidently. If they don’t, treat the email as ambiguous — ask the sender to clarify rather than picking a destination and hoping.
  • Nothing in the queue should silently fall through. Every inbound gets a routing decision (resolve at Tier 1, forward, redirect, or hold-and-clarify) within whatever response window your team operates on. The most common Helpdesk Tech failure mode is an email sitting unread because no one was sure what to do with it.

Part 2 · Routing Playbook

The nine inbound types — what each is, and what you do

One section per inbound type, in roughly the order of frequency you’ll see them. Each section gives you the recognition pattern, the routing action, and the why — so when an email doesn’t look quite like the template you can still make the right call.

Inbound type 1 of 9

Pre-install submission — new customer install trigger

From
Customer I/T — usually the named contact in the customer’s SUBMITTED BY block.
Subject
RapidReconciler Pre-Install Submission — {Customer Name}
Body shape
Long structured plaintext with TOPOLOGY SELECTED, SOURCE PLATFORM, SERVER NAMES & INTERNAL IPs, CHECKLIST SUMMARY, CHECKLIST DETAIL, JDE DATA DICTIONARY VARIABLES, JDE CONNECTION DETAILS, and SUBMITTED BY sections. Generated from the Tech Installation Prep guide on the customer side.
Frequency
One per new customer install — rare individually but the most consequential email in the queue.

What it actually is: the customer has finished the pre-install checklist and is telling GSI their environment is ready. This email triggers the database-side install workflow.

Route to RR DBA

How to route: forward the email to RR DBA. The DBA will gate-check the submission for completeness (30 of 30 checklist items, topology selected, all server names and IPs populated) before scheduling the install meeting. If they bounce it back asking for re-submission, that’s expected; reply to the customer with whatever the DBA flagged.

Why this matters

This is one of two emails where mis-routing causes structural delay. Sending it to RR Network Tech instead of RR DBA means the database install never starts, the customer waits, and eventually someone realises and reroutes. Recognising the subject line is the fastest path to getting it right.

Inbound type 2 of 9

Database-ready notification — VALC install trigger

From
RR DBA (an internal GSI tech-team member).
Subject
RapidReconciler Database Ready — {Customer Name} — VALC Install Can Begin
Body shape
Short structured email naming the customer, listing any non-default values (SQL port, rruser password) the VALC tech needs, confirming the initial load was verified by Customer I/T, and including a topology-filtered pre-flight checklist with all items confirmed.
Frequency
One per new install (the same customer that submitted the pre-install email some days earlier).

What it actually is: the database side of the install is complete and the VALC side can begin. The DBA has been waiting for the customer’s confirmation that the initial data load succeeded, so this email implicitly signals that gate is cleared.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. They’ll start the VALC install (creating the client record, agent install, licensing) using both this email and the customer’s original pre-install submission — both are inputs.

Pair these two emails together

RR Network Tech needs both the original Pre-Install Submission email and this Database-Ready notification to do the VALC install. The customer-submission part has the topology, server IPs, and JDE details; this part adds the SQL port (if non-default) and rruser password (if non-default). When you forward, link the two messages so the network tech doesn’t hunt.

Inbound type 3 of 9

Password policy activation request

From
Customer I/T — usually the customer’s designated RapidReconciler administrator or their I/T lead.
Pattern
Asks to enable the complex password policy. Should include company name, the name of the customer’s RR administrator, and a requested activation date.
Frequency
Occasional — usually post-go-live as customers tighten their security posture.

What it actually is: the customer wants the complex password policy turned on for their client (8-char minimum, complexity rules, 90-day expiry, 10-password history). RR Network Tech flips a per-customer toggle in VALC; once enabled, every existing user is forced to reset at next login.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. If the email is missing any of the three required pieces of information (company name, RR administrator name, activation date), reply to the customer asking for them before forwarding — the network tech needs all three to action the request.

What the customer should know: existing users will be required to reset their passwords at next login once the policy is active. The Password Policy reference on the tech KB recommends notifying users before activation. If their email doesn’t indicate they’ve done this, it’s polite to mention it in your reply.

Inbound type 4 of 9

License renewal trigger

From
Sales (typically the AE or renewal owner).
Pattern
Confirms a renewal contract has been signed and asks tech to update the License End Date in VALC.
Frequency
Annual per customer, sometimes batched at quarter-ends.

What it actually is: the renewal commercial work is done; tech needs to push the License End Date forward in VALC so users don’t get locked out at the old expiry. The lockout is enforced by VALC — missing this update is a customer-facing outage.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. Include the customer name and the new License End Date if they’re obvious from the email. If the renewal also adds company licenses or modules, that’s a separate change but goes to the same person — RR Network Tech handles all live-customer changes.

Inbound type 5 of 9

Additional database request (Test / Dev / Train)

From
Customer I/T.
Pattern
Existing live customer asks to add a Test, Dev, or Training RapidReconciler database alongside their Production database.
Frequency
Occasional, usually as customers grow into the product.

What it actually is: the customer wants another RapidReconciler database registered — e.g. for testing changes before applying to Prod. RR Network Tech coordinates with the customer’s DBA on the backup-restore (existing scripts can’t be re-run because they’re hard-coded for Prod) and then registers the new database in VALC.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech with the customer name and which kind of database they’re asking for. The network tech will reply to the customer with the backup-restore guidance.

Inbound type 6 of 9

Live-customer change request (everything else)

From
Customer I/T.
Pattern
Anything live-customer-related not covered above: adding company licenses, enabling additional module tabs, updating database connection details (IP, port, credentials), deactivating a client, requesting a Services or Agent push, asking about a server migration.
Frequency
The bulk of inbound from existing customers.

What it actually is: ongoing operational work for an existing live customer. RR Network Tech’s Common Requests reference is the ownership matrix — some of these are GSI tasks, others get redirected to the customer’s own RR administrator.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. They’ll either action it or, for user-management requests (adding/removing users, password resets, permission changes), redirect the customer to their own designated administrator. Don’t try to make the redirect call yourself — the boundary between “GSI handles this” and “Customer Admin handles this” is in the Common Requests reference and RR Network Tech reads it daily.

Don’t escalate users directly

Server migrations, large-scale infrastructure changes, or anything that sounds urgent — still routes to RR Network Tech, not directly to RR DBA. The network tech is the right first read on whether something needs DBA escalation. Going around them creates duplicate work and missed context.

Inbound type 7 of 9

Documentation feedback

From
Anyone — customer, GSI staff, even other readers.
Pattern
Subject often includes "Documentation Feedback" (the KB feedback links pre-fill subject lines like GSI RR Sales - Documentation Feedback). Body references a specific page or section with a correction, missing step, or suggested improvement.
Frequency
Occasional. Spikes after a doc revision goes out.

What it actually is: someone spotted an error, missing step, or out-of-date procedure in the KB and is reporting it through the official channel.

Handle in mailbox

How to handle: log the feedback in whatever the team uses for doc-improvement tracking, then reply acknowledging receipt. If the feedback names a specific role (“the install procedure says X but actually does Y”), you can also forward to that role’s owner so they see it directly — but the master record stays with you.

Inbound type 8 of 9

Generic customer support questions

From
End users at customer companies, sometimes with no I/T context attached.
Pattern
Typical examples: “I’ve been locked out, can you reset my password?”, “Why don’t I see company X?”, “The data looks wrong this morning.”
Frequency
Variable — can spike after maintenance windows or version pushes.

What it actually is: usually one of two things. Either it’s a user-administration issue (“reset my password”, “give me access to module X”) which belongs with the customer’s own RR administrator, or it’s a real product issue that needs a tech response.

Triage

How to triage:

  • User-administration questions (passwords, access to modules, adding/removing users) — reply pointing the user to their own customer-side RR administrator. This is documented in the Common Requests reference: GSI does not handle user administration on the customer’s behalf unless explicitly contracted.
  • Product / data issues — forward to RR Network Tech as a live-customer change request (see type 6). The network tech is the first read; they’ll escalate to RR DBA if the diagnosis points at the database side.
  • Can’t tell which — reply asking the user to clarify whether they’ve already tried their internal RR administrator, and what specifically isn’t working. Treat as ambiguous (next section).
Draft — manager to validate

The right reply template for redirecting end-users to their own RR administrator probably exists somewhere, but it isn’t in the source documentation. Check with your manager for the canonical wording before relying on free-form replies.

Inbound type 9 of 9

Ambiguous / unclear emails

Pattern
Subject and body don’t clearly identify the message type. Could be a customer asking something cryptic, an internal forward without context, or a reply to an old thread that’s lost the original subject.
Frequency
Less common than you’d expect, but it happens.

What it actually is: you don’t know yet. That’s the whole point of this category — pretending you know is the failure mode.

Hold & clarify

How to handle: reply asking for the specific information you need to classify the email correctly. Concrete examples:

  • If it’s a customer email referencing their environment but missing the company name, ask for the company name.
  • If it looks like a change request but doesn’t say what’s changing, ask what they’re trying to do.
  • If it’s an internal forward with no original context, ask the GSI sender what they’d like done with it.

What not to do: don’t guess and forward. The receiving tech will reroute, the customer waits longer, and you’ve lost the chance to get the routing right on the first pass.

Don’t let ambiguous threads stall

The biggest failure mode for the Helpdesk Tech role is an ambiguous email sitting unread because no one was sure what to do with it. “I’ll come back to this” turns into a week. Reply asking for clarification today, even if the reply is one sentence — the moment something is in your queue, it should have either been routed, handled, or have an outstanding clarification request out.

Part 3 · Tier 1 Support

What Tier 1 covers

Beyond routing, the Helpdesk Tech is the first-line responder for inbound support. A meaningful portion of what arrives in rrsupport@getgsi.com doesn’t need a DBA or Network Tech — it needs someone who can read the question, gather a few details, and either resolve it or hand a clean ticket to the right person.

Draft — manager to validate

The specifics of what counts as Tier 1 at GSI — which tools you have access to, which fixes you’re authorised to apply directly, what response-time targets apply, and the boundary between Tier 1 and Tier 2 escalation — are team-specific and aren’t fully captured in the source documentation. Confirm the scope and tooling with your manager before your first shift. The guidance below is a working baseline.

Typical Tier 1 work

  • Answering “how do I…” questions — pointing customers and end users to the right KB page or walking them through a documented procedure they’re missing.
  • Information gathering — on any inbound that’s missing details (customer name, environment, error message, screenshots, timestamps), getting the gaps filled before the ticket lands with a tech who’d otherwise have to ask.
  • Login and access basics — verifying who the customer’s own RapidReconciler administrator is and redirecting end-user account questions there. Most user-management questions are not GSI’s to handle.
  • Known-issue lookups — recognising repeat questions and replying with the standard answer; flagging anything you suspect is a new pattern so the team can decide whether it belongs in the KB.
  • Status replies — acknowledging receipt, confirming a ticket has been routed, and giving the customer a realistic next-step expectation.

Out of Tier 1 scope

  • Database changes, schema work, SSIS-package edits — RR DBA.
  • VALC-side configuration, agent rollout, certificate-renewal steps 2–3 — RR Network Tech.
  • Backend / web-interface configuration, certificate-renewal Step 1 — RR UI Developer.
  • Schema bugs or product-code issues — RR Developer.
  • Anything that requires touching a customer’s production environment.

Tier 1 workflow

For every inbound that doesn’t fit the structured-routing patterns in Part 2, the loop is the same:

  1. Acknowledge. Reply to the sender so they know the request landed and is being looked at. A one-line acknowledgement is enough — silence is the worst outcome.
  2. Classify. Decide: is this Tier 1, or is it past Tier 1? If past, skip to step 5.
  3. Gather. If you’re missing details needed to resolve or escalate cleanly — customer name, environment, what they tried, error text, screenshots — ask in the first reply. Asking for everything at once is faster than three back-and-forths.
  4. Resolve. Apply the standard answer, point to the KB page, or walk through the documented step. Reply with what you did and confirm the customer is unblocked.
  5. Escalate. If it’s past Tier 1 (or you’ve tried Tier 1 and it didn’t resolve), forward to the right tech with the gathered context attached — symptoms, what you’ve already tried, customer details. Don’t make the tech re-gather what you already have.
  6. Close the loop. Once the receiving tech responds or resolves, make sure the customer hears back. Either the tech replies directly, or you confirm resolution and close.
Replies should set expectations

Customers don’t mind waiting if they know they’ve been heard. They mind not knowing whether anyone read the email. Every Tier 1 acknowledgement should say what happens next: “I’ve forwarded this to our DBA team and you’ll hear back today,” or “here’s the answer, let us know if it doesn’t resolve it.” Open-ended “we’re looking at it” replies are the worst of both worlds.

When to escalate

Escalate to the appropriate tech when any of the following are true:

  • The fix requires touching a database, VALC server, backend, or production customer environment.
  • The customer is reporting an error or behaviour you can’t reproduce against the documented expected behaviour, and the KB doesn’t cover it.
  • You’ve tried the standard Tier 1 fix and it didn’t resolve the issue.
  • The issue affects an installed customer’s ability to use RapidReconciler (anything that looks customer-down or customer-degraded escalates immediately, not at the end of your shift).
  • You’re unsure whether something is Tier 1 or not. A clean escalation that turns out to have been Tier 1 is cheap; a stalled Tier 1 attempt that should have been escalated is expensive.

Where to escalate

The structured-routing patterns in Part 2 cover where the trigger-emails go. For Tier 1 escalation specifically:

  • Database, schema, SSIS, data load, rruser account issues → RR DBA.
  • VALC issues, agent issues, web-interface access from inside customer network, certificate-renewal Steps 2–3 → RR Network Tech.
  • Backend / web-interface configuration, certificate-renewal Step 1 → RR UI Developer.
  • Suspected product bug, schema change request, behaviour the docs say should work but doesn’t → RR Developer.
  • Sales / contractual / renewal questions you shouldn’t be answering → redirect the customer to their Sales contact, copying the relevant Sales person if known.
Escalate with context

The single biggest favour you can do the receiving tech is escalate with everything they need to start work: customer name, environment, what the customer reported, what you tried at Tier 1 and the outcome, error text or screenshots, and any timestamps. A clean escalation saves the tech a 20-minute back-and-forth with the customer.

How to escalate — two paths

For customer-support escalations specifically (database / VALC / agent / product-bug categories above), the Troubleshooting Hub provides two ready-made handoffs from inside any matching scenario card. Both pre-fill the standard escalation template with the symptom, steps already tried, and the next-up contact — you don’t need to write the handoff from scratch.

  • Reply-all on the customer’s email using the Copy escalation summary button on the card. Best for fast-moving conversations or when the customer is on the phone — everything stays in one thread.
  • Create a JSM ticket using the Create JSM ticket button on the card. Best for issues that need formal tracking, span shifts, or have SLA implications. The ticket lands in the RapidReconciler Support project as a Support issue with the troubleshooting context auto-filled.

See the Customer support workflow section above for the full walkthrough of both paths, including what the JSM dialog looks like.

Wrap-up

What to do next

You’ve walked the mailbox, seen all nine inbound types, and reviewed the Tier 1 support loop. Three suggested next steps before your first shift:

  1. Read the two trigger-email examples in the source docs. The Pre-Install Submission email is shown in full in Installing the Production Database; the Database-Ready notification template is in that same doc’s Step 12. Seeing the actual structured content makes the routing patterns instantaneous to recognise later.
  2. Read the Common Requests reference on the VALC client-install page. It’s the ownership matrix RR Network Tech uses to decide GSI-handles vs Customer-Admin-handles. Knowing the boundaries means your replies to user-admin questions land more confidently.
  3. Open the Roles & Responsibilities doc and skim RR DBA and RR Network Tech’s profiles. You’re routing to these two people most of the time — knowing what they actually own and don’t own makes routing decisions much faster.
If you have time before going solo

Before handling the mailbox without backup, ask to shadow an experienced Helpdesk Tech for a handful of customer-support tickets. The judgment call between “resolve at Tier 1” and “escalate now” is hard to learn from a guide; it’s easy to learn from watching someone make the call a few times. Not a hard requirement — some teams skip this if their inbound volume doesn’t warrant it — but worth asking about.
