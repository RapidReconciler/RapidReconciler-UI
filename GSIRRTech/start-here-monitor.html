Part 1 · Mailbox Tour

What the mailbox is — and what it isn’t

rrsupport@getgsi.com is the single inbound address listed across every GSI RapidReconciler document — the one customers, sales, and the tech team all write to. As Monitor, you don’t do the technical work of installs, migrations, or fixes. You read every inbound message, identify what it is, and route it to the right person on the tech team.

What the mailbox is

  • The single trigger for the tech-side install. Customer I/T submits their pre-install email here; that email is what kicks off RR DBA's database install.
  • The seam between the database install and the VALC install. RR DBA sends a database-ready notification here when their work is done; that email is what kicks off RR Network Tech's VALC install.
  • The customer’s primary contact point for change requests, password-policy activations, license renewals, and ad-hoc support questions across every live customer.
  • The KB’s feedback channel. Every documentation page ends with a feedback band pointing here for corrections.

What the mailbox isn’t

  • It isn’t a ticketing system. Treat it as a triage queue: you read, you classify, you forward, you note what you did. If something needs structured tracking, push that through whatever your team uses for tickets — the mailbox itself isn’t it.
  • It isn’t the place where work gets done. Once you route an email to RR DBA or RR Network Tech, your involvement on that thread is complete unless they bounce it back to you.
  • It isn’t a place to make technical decisions. If you’re unsure whether something is a database issue or a VALC issue, route it where the symptoms point and let the receiving tech reroute if needed. Don’t try to diagnose.
Draft — manager to validate

This guide doesn’t cover specifics like response-time SLAs, ticketing-tool integration, shift handoff procedures, or out-of-office coverage. Confirm those with your manager before your first shift — they’re team-specific and not in the source documentation.

Who sends what to this mailbox

Five sources put email into rrsupport@getgsi.com. Recognising the source quickly is half the job — the other half is recognising the message type within that source.

  • Customer I/T — the customer's own technical staff. They send the pre-install submission that triggers a new install, plus password-policy activation requests and ad-hoc change requests over a customer’s lifetime.
  • RR DBA — a GSI tech-team member. Sends one specific email, the database-ready notification, that hands a customer off to VALC. Every install produces exactly one of these.
  • Sales — usually only when a renewal or scope change has been agreed and they’re looping in the tech team to update VALC.
  • End users at customer companies — occasionally. Most user-management questions should go to the customer’s own RapidReconciler administrator; if they reach this mailbox, you’ll usually redirect.
  • Anyone reading the KB — documentation-feedback emails come from the feedback band on every page.

Three principles to internalise

  • Read the subject first. The two structurally important emails — the pre-install submission and the database-ready notification — have predictable subject-line formats. Recognising those lets you route in seconds. The full subject patterns are in the routing playbook below.
  • Route on signal, not on guess. If the subject and body clearly identify the type, route confidently. If they don’t, treat the email as ambiguous — ask the sender to clarify rather than picking a destination and hoping.
  • Nothing in the queue should silently fall through. Every inbound gets a routing decision (forward, redirect, handle, or hold-and-clarify) within whatever response window your team operates on. The most common Monitor failure mode is an email sitting unread because no one was sure what to do with it.

Part 2 · Routing Playbook

The nine inbound types — what each is, and what you do

One section per inbound type, in roughly the order of frequency you’ll see them. Each section gives you the recognition pattern, the routing action, and the why — so when an email doesn’t look quite like the template you can still make the right call.

Inbound type 1 of 9

Pre-install submission — new customer install trigger

From
Customer I/T — usually the named contact in the customer’s SUBMITTED BY block.
Subject
RapidReconciler Pre-Install Submission — {Customer Name}
Body shape
Long structured plaintext with TOPOLOGY SELECTED, SOURCE PLATFORM, SERVER NAMES & INTERNAL IPs, CHECKLIST SUMMARY, CHECKLIST DETAIL, JDE DATA DICTIONARY VARIABLES, JDE CONNECTION DETAILS, and SUBMITTED BY sections. Generated from the Tech Installation Prep guide on the customer side.
Frequency
One per new customer install — rare individually but the most consequential email in the queue.

What it actually is: the customer has finished the pre-install checklist and is telling GSI their environment is ready. This email triggers the database-side install workflow.

Route to RR DBA

How to route: forward the email to RR DBA. The DBA will gate-check the submission for completeness (30 of 30 checklist items, topology selected, all server names and IPs populated) before scheduling the install meeting. If they bounce it back asking for re-submission, that’s expected; reply to the customer with whatever the DBA flagged.

Why this matters

This is one of two emails where mis-routing causes structural delay. Sending it to RR Network Tech instead of RR DBA means the database install never starts, the customer waits, and eventually someone realises and reroutes. Recognising the subject line is the fastest path to getting it right.

Inbound type 2 of 9

Database-ready notification — VALC install trigger

From
RR DBA (an internal GSI tech-team member).
Subject
RapidReconciler Database Ready — {Customer Name} — VALC Install Can Begin
Body shape
Short structured email naming the customer, listing any non-default values (SQL port, rruser password) the VALC tech needs, confirming the initial load was verified by Customer I/T, and including a topology-filtered pre-flight checklist with all items confirmed.
Frequency
One per new install (the same customer that submitted the pre-install email some days earlier).

What it actually is: the database side of the install is complete and the VALC side can begin. The DBA has been waiting for the customer’s confirmation that the initial data load succeeded, so this email implicitly signals that gate is cleared.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. They’ll start the VALC install (creating the client record, agent install, licensing) using both this email and the customer’s original pre-install submission — both are inputs.

Pair these two emails together

RR Network Tech needs both the original Pre-Install Submission email and this Database-Ready notification to do the VALC install. The customer-submission part has the topology, server IPs, and JDE details; this part adds the SQL port (if non-default) and rruser password (if non-default). When you forward, link the two messages so the network tech doesn’t hunt.

Inbound type 3 of 9

Password policy activation request

From
Customer I/T — usually the customer’s designated RapidReconciler administrator or their I/T lead.
Pattern
Asks to enable the complex password policy. Should include company name, the name of the customer’s RR administrator, and a requested activation date.
Frequency
Occasional — usually post-go-live as customers tighten their security posture.

What it actually is: the customer wants the complex password policy turned on for their client (8-char minimum, complexity rules, 90-day expiry, 10-password history). RR Network Tech flips a per-customer toggle in VALC; once enabled, every existing user is forced to reset at next login.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. If the email is missing any of the three required pieces of information (company name, RR administrator name, activation date), reply to the customer asking for them before forwarding — the network tech needs all three to action the request.

What the customer should know: existing users will be required to reset their passwords at next login once the policy is active. The Password Policy reference on the tech KB recommends notifying users before activation. If their email doesn’t indicate they’ve done this, it’s polite to mention it in your reply.

Inbound type 4 of 9

License renewal trigger

From
Sales (typically the AE or renewal owner).
Pattern
Confirms a renewal contract has been signed and asks tech to update the License End Date in VALC.
Frequency
Annual per customer, sometimes batched at quarter-ends.

What it actually is: the renewal commercial work is done; tech needs to push the License End Date forward in VALC so users don’t get locked out at the old expiry. The lockout is enforced by VALC — missing this update is a customer-facing outage.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. Include the customer name and the new License End Date if they’re obvious from the email. If the renewal also adds company licenses or modules, that’s a separate change but goes to the same person — RR Network Tech handles all live-customer changes.

Inbound type 5 of 9

Additional database request (Test / Dev / Train)

From
Customer I/T.
Pattern
Existing live customer asks to add a Test, Dev, or Training RapidReconciler database alongside their Production database.
Frequency
Occasional, usually as customers grow into the product.

What it actually is: the customer wants another RapidReconciler database registered — e.g. for testing changes before applying to Prod. RR Network Tech coordinates with the customer’s DBA on the backup-restore (existing scripts can’t be re-run because they’re hard-coded for Prod) and then registers the new database in VALC.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech with the customer name and which kind of database they’re asking for. The network tech will reply to the customer with the backup-restore guidance.

Inbound type 6 of 9

Live-customer change request (everything else)

From
Customer I/T.
Pattern
Anything live-customer-related not covered above: adding company licenses, enabling additional module tabs, updating database connection details (IP, port, credentials), deactivating a client, requesting a Services or Agent push, asking about a server migration.
Frequency
The bulk of inbound from existing customers.

What it actually is: ongoing operational work for an existing live customer. RR Network Tech’s Common Requests reference is the ownership matrix — some of these are GSI tasks, others get redirected to the customer’s own RR administrator.

Route to RR Network Tech

How to route: forward to RR Network Tech. They’ll either action it or, for user-management requests (adding/removing users, password resets, permission changes), redirect the customer to their own designated administrator. Don’t try to make the redirect call yourself — the boundary between “GSI handles this” and “Customer Admin handles this” is in the Common Requests reference and RR Network Tech reads it daily.

Don’t escalate users directly

Server migrations, large-scale infrastructure changes, or anything that sounds urgent — still routes to RR Network Tech, not directly to RR DBA. The network tech is the right first read on whether something needs DBA escalation. Going around them creates duplicate work and missed context.

Inbound type 7 of 9

Documentation feedback

From
Anyone — customer, GSI staff, even other readers.
Pattern
Subject often includes "Documentation Feedback" (the KB feedback links pre-fill subject lines like GSI RR Sales - Documentation Feedback). Body references a specific page or section with a correction, missing step, or suggested improvement.
Frequency
Occasional. Spikes after a doc revision goes out.

What it actually is: someone spotted an error, missing step, or out-of-date procedure in the KB and is reporting it through the official channel.

Handle in mailbox

How to handle: log the feedback in whatever the team uses for doc-improvement tracking, then reply acknowledging receipt. If the feedback names a specific role (“the install procedure says X but actually does Y”), you can also forward to that role’s owner so they see it directly — but the master record stays with you.

Inbound type 8 of 9

Generic customer support questions

From
End users at customer companies, sometimes with no I/T context attached.
Pattern
Typical examples: “I’ve been locked out, can you reset my password?”, “Why don’t I see company X?”, “The data looks wrong this morning.”
Frequency
Variable — can spike after maintenance windows or version pushes.

What it actually is: usually one of two things. Either it’s a user-administration issue (“reset my password”, “give me access to module X”) which belongs with the customer’s own RR administrator, or it’s a real product issue that needs a tech response.

Triage

How to triage:

  • User-administration questions (passwords, access to modules, adding/removing users) — reply pointing the user to their own customer-side RR administrator. This is documented in the Common Requests reference: GSI does not handle user administration on the customer’s behalf unless explicitly contracted.
  • Product / data issues — forward to RR Network Tech as a live-customer change request (see type 6). The network tech is the first read; they’ll escalate to RR DBA if the diagnosis points at the database side.
  • Can’t tell which — reply asking the user to clarify whether they’ve already tried their internal RR administrator, and what specifically isn’t working. Treat as ambiguous (next section).
Draft — manager to validate

The right reply template for redirecting end-users to their own RR administrator probably exists somewhere, but it isn’t in the source documentation. Check with your manager for the canonical wording before relying on free-form replies.

Inbound type 9 of 9

Ambiguous / unclear emails

Pattern
Subject and body don’t clearly identify the message type. Could be a customer asking something cryptic, an internal forward without context, or a reply to an old thread that’s lost the original subject.
Frequency
Less common than you’d expect, but it happens.

What it actually is: you don’t know yet. That’s the whole point of this category — pretending you know is the failure mode.

Hold & clarify

How to handle: reply asking for the specific information you need to classify the email correctly. Concrete examples:

  • If it’s a customer email referencing their environment but missing the company name, ask for the company name.
  • If it looks like a change request but doesn’t say what’s changing, ask what they’re trying to do.
  • If it’s an internal forward with no original context, ask the GSI sender what they’d like done with it.

What not to do: don’t guess and forward. The receiving tech will reroute, the customer waits longer, and you’ve lost the chance to get the routing right on the first pass.

Don’t let ambiguous threads stall

The biggest failure mode for the Monitor role is an ambiguous email sitting unread because no one was sure what to do with it. “I’ll come back to this” turns into a week. Reply asking for clarification today, even if the reply is one sentence — the moment something is in your queue, it should have either been routed, handled, or have an outstanding clarification request out.

Wrap-up

What to do next

You’ve walked the mailbox and seen all nine inbound types. Three suggested next steps before your first shift:

  1. Read the two trigger-email examples in the source docs. The Pre-Install Submission email is shown in full in Installing the Production Database; the Database-Ready notification template is in that same doc’s Step 12. Seeing the actual structured content makes the routing patterns instantaneous to recognise later.
  2. Read the Common Requests reference on the VALC client-install page. It’s the ownership matrix RR Network Tech uses to decide GSI-handles vs Customer-Admin-handles. Knowing the boundaries means your replies to user-admin questions land more confidently.
  3. Open the Roles & Responsibilities doc and skim RR DBA and RR Network Tech’s profiles. You’re routing to these two people most of the time — knowing what they actually own and don’t own makes routing decisions much faster.
